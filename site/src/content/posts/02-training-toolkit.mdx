## "It learns" is not "it learns fast"

Module 01 ended in triumph and a confession. The triumph: backpropagation, derived by
hand, trained a network that cleared the XOR wall and went on to hit 97% on MNIST. The
confession was buried in the details — a learning rate we picked by feel (`0.5`), a weight
init drawn from a plain uniform `[-1, 1)`, and saturating sigmoids. On a 2→4→1 toy none of
that bites. Make the network deeper and every one of those choices turns from harmless to
fatal: the sigmoids flatten out and stop passing gradient, the wrong init scale makes the
signal explode or vanish before it reaches the end, and plain gradient descent crawls along
curved valleys it should cut straight across.

This chapter is the **toolkit** the field assembled, mostly between 1998 and 2015, to fix
exactly those failures — and make training something you can *trust* instead of *tune*.
There is no single grand idea here like backprop; there are four small, sharp ones, and each
is only a few lines of code on top of what we already have:

| Problem in Module 01 | Fix | Who |
|----------------------|-----|-----|
| Sigmoids saturate → gradient dies | **ReLU** (and friends) | Nair & Hinton 2010 |
| Wrong init scale → signal explodes/vanishes | **Xavier / He** initialization | Glorot 2010 · He 2015 |
| One global learning rate, slow valleys | **momentum → RMSProp → Adam** | Polyak 1964 · Hinton 2012 · Kingma 2014 |
| Big net memorizes small data | **L2 weight decay · early stopping** | — |

And because these pieces recur in every module from here on, this is where we stop writing
one-off networks and stand up a small shared library: **`nanograd`**. It is *not* an autograd
engine — every layer's backward pass is still the Module-01 chain rule, written out by hand.
We are just packaging what we already understand so we can reuse it.

## Idea 1 — activations: the slope is the whole story

An activation function does two jobs. Forward, it bends the linear map into a curve. Backward,
its **derivative multiplies every gradient that flows through it** — so wherever that slope is
near zero, learning silently stops. That one sentence explains the shift from sigmoid to ReLU.

$$
\sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)\,(1-\sigma(z)) \le \tfrac14, \qquad \tanh'(z) = 1 - \tanh^2 z, \qquad \mathrm{ReLU}'(z) = \mathbb{1}[z > 0].
$$

Sigmoid's derivative peaks at a mere `0.25` and collapses toward `0` once `|z| ≳ 4` — its
neurons **saturate**, and a saturated neuron is a closed gate. Stack a few layers of them and
the product of those small slopes drives the gradient to nothing (the *vanishing gradient*).
ReLU's positive-side slope is a flat **1**, however large `z` grows: the gate stays open. That
single property, more than anything else, is what later let networks go genuinely deep.

## Idea 2 — initialization: keep the signal alive

If activations decide whether a *single* layer passes gradient, initialization decides whether
the *whole stack* does. Push a random input through a deep network and track the standard
deviation of the activations layer by layer. Too-large initial weights and it grows without
bound (and tanh saturates); too-small and it decays toward zero. Either way, the gradient —
which rides the same scale on the way back — is useless before it reaches the front.

Glorot & Bengio's fix (2010) is almost embarrassingly simple: scale the initial weights by the
**fan-in** so the variance stays put from layer to layer.

$$
\textbf{Xavier (tanh/sigmoid): } \operatorname{Var}(W) = \frac{1}{n_\text{in}} \qquad\qquad \textbf{He (ReLU): } \operatorname{Var}(W) = \frac{2}{n_\text{in}}.
$$

He's extra factor of `2` accounts for ReLU zeroing out half its inputs. In the notebook you
can watch a 6-layer tanh stack's activation std stay flat under Xavier and quietly die under
the Module-01 `[-1,1)` uniform — the same plot Glorot used to make the case.

## Idea 3 — optimizers: give the step a memory

Plain SGD, $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\,g$, treats every parameter the same and remembers
nothing. Three refinements, each a couple of lines on the last, fix its worst habits:

$$
\textbf{momentum: } v \leftarrow \mu v + g,\ \ \theta \mathrel{-}= \eta v \qquad \textbf{RMSProp: } s \leftarrow \beta s + (1-\beta)g^2,\ \ \theta \mathrel{-}= \frac{\eta\,g}{\sqrt{s}+\epsilon}
$$

Momentum accumulates a velocity, so consistent directions build speed and oscillations across
a valley cancel. RMSProp divides each coordinate's step by a running RMS of *its own* gradient
— a per-parameter learning rate that shrinks where gradients are wild and grows where they're
timid. **Adam** (Kingma & Ba 2014) is simply *both at once*, plus a bias-correction so the
running averages aren't dragged toward their zero start:

$$
m \leftarrow \beta_1 m + (1-\beta_1)g,\quad v \leftarrow \beta_2 v + (1-\beta_2)g^2,\quad \hat m = \frac{m}{1-\beta_1^{\,t}},\ \hat v = \frac{v}{1-\beta_2^{\,t}},\quad \theta \mathrel{-}= \frac{\eta\,\hat m}{\sqrt{\hat v}+\epsilon}.
$$

It is the default optimizer of modern deep learning, and it is *nine lines of arithmetic*.

## Idea 4 — regularization: trade a little training loss for generalization

Give a big network a small, noisy dataset and it will memorize it — training loss keeps
falling while validation loss turns back up. **L2 weight decay** adds
$\tfrac{\lambda}{2}\lVert\theta\rVert^2$ to the loss, i.e. a $\lambda\theta$ term on every
gradient, pulling weights toward zero so the fit stays smooth; it is one keyword,
`weight_decay`, on any of the optimizers above. **Early stopping** just halts at the
validation minimum. Neither is glamorous; both are always on in practice.

## The code: from scratch, and still in lockstep

Everything above lives in [`lib/*/nanograd`](https://github.com/shaia/deep-learning-from-scratch/tree/main/lib).
The refactor changes nothing about the math — a `Linear` layer's `backward` is the same
`dW = Xᵀ dY` we derived last time — it just gives each operation a home. Here is the whole Adam
step, the module's centerpiece, side by side. The project's contract (pedagogy principle #3)
still holds: **C and Python agree to ~14 decimal places** — well inside the same `1e-9` gate
as Module 01 — now even through 2000 Adam updates.

```python
# lib/python/nanograd/optim.py — Adam.step, over parallel param/grad lists
self.t += 1
b1c = 1.0 - self.beta1 ** self.t          # bias-correction denominators
b2c = 1.0 - self.beta2 ** self.t
for i, (p, g) in enumerate(zip(params, grads)):
    self.m[i] = self.beta1 * self.m[i] + (1.0 - self.beta1) * g
    self.v[i] = self.beta2 * self.v[i] + (1.0 - self.beta2) * (g * g)
    m_hat = self.m[i] / b1c
    v_hat = self.v[i] / b2c
    p -= self.lr * m_hat / (np.sqrt(v_hat) + self.eps)   # in-place update
```

```c
/* lib/c/nanograd/optim.c — ng_adam_step; b1c/b2c passed in, tracked by the caller */
for (int i = 0; i < n; i++) {
    double gi = g[i];
    m[i] = beta1 * m[i] + (1.0 - beta1) * gi;
    v[i] = beta2 * v[i] + (1.0 - beta2) * (gi * gi);
    double m_hat = m[i] / b1c;
    double v_hat = v[i] / b2c;
    p[i] -= lr * m_hat / (sqrt(v_hat) + eps);
}
```

The agreement test trains the identical `2→8→2` ReLU/He network on XOR in both languages and
matches every parameter to about `1e-14` — comfortably inside the `1e-9` gate. (The only drift
is last-bit rounding in `exp`/`log`/`sqrt` across the two math libraries, exactly as in Module
01.) The notebook then goes further and checks our optimizer against the real thing:

> Our `nanograd.Adam` and `torch.optim.Adam`, run from the same weights on the same gradients,
> agree to a relative error of **`1e-16`** — they are the same nine lines. And every gradient
> our hand-written layers produce matches PyTorch's autograd to `~1e-14`. There is only one
> right answer, and the from-scratch code lands on it.

## Toy to real: the toolkit on MNIST

XOR proves the machinery; MNIST proves it scales. The same `784 → 128 → 10` network as Module
01, now with **ReLU/He** and trained by **Adam** — no learning-rate babysitting — crosses 95%
in a couple of epochs and settles near **96.5%** at eight. The
[notebook](https://github.com/shaia/deep-learning-from-scratch/blob/main/topics/02-training-toolkit/notebook.ipynb)
trains it end to end, then rebuilds the same net idiomatically in TensorFlow (a `GradientTape`
+ Adam) and Keras (He init, Adam, and an L2 penalty in three lines), which lands at 97%.
