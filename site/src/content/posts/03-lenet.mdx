## The machine that read your checks

In 1998, if you deposited a check in the United States, there was a fair chance that no human
being ever read the amount. A machine did — and inside that machine was a neural network. AT&T's
check-reading system, built at Bell Labs around a network called **LeNet-5** and deployed
commercially through NCR, was processing millions of checks a day by the late 1990s; Yann LeCun's
own estimate is that at its peak the system was reading somewhere between 10 and 20 percent of
all the checks written in America. Handwritten digits, scrawled by millions of strangers in every
imaginable style, classified by a learned model at industrial scale and commercial accuracy — a
decade before "deep learning" was a phrase anyone used.

It is worth pausing on why *checks*, of all things. America in the 1990s wrote tens of billions
of them a year, and every single one had a handwritten "courtesy amount" that somebody — before
this system, an actual person at a keyboard — had to read and key in. Template matching, the
classical answer, dies instantly against real handwriting: there is no template for the way ten
million strangers write a 2. The task demanded a model that *learns* what digits look like from
examples, tolerates sloppy placement and wild style variation, and does it fast enough and
cheaply enough to sit inside a bank's nightly pipeline. In other words: it demanded everything
this book has built so far, plus one missing idea about images.

This chapter builds that network. Not because digit-reading is still the frontier — it isn't —
but because LeNet-5 is where the single most important architectural idea in computer vision
enters the story: the **convolution**. Modules 01 and 02 gave us a network that can learn and a
toolkit that makes it learn fast. But we have been feeding it images as `784` loose, unrelated
numbers — throwing away the one thing everybody knows about pictures, which is that *nearby
pixels belong together*. Convolution is what you get when you stop throwing that away. It is a
small idea — embarrassingly small, once you see it — and it is the backbone of everything from
this chapter to the vision transformers that eventually challenged it.

## From cat cortex to zip codes

The idea did not start in engineering. It started in a darkened lab at Johns Hopkins in 1959,
with an anesthetized cat watching spots of light.

David Hubel and Torsten Wiesel were recording from single neurons in the cat's primary visual
cortex, trying to find what made them fire. Spots and flashes did almost nothing. The
breakthrough came, famously, half by accident: as they slid a glass slide into their projector,
its faint moving *edge* swept across the screen — and a neuron crackled to life. What that neuron
wanted, it turned out, was an edge, at a particular *orientation*, in a particular *place* in the
visual field. They mapped cell after cell and found a division of labor that should sound
familiar by the end of this chapter. **Simple cells** fired for an oriented edge at one exact
position. **Complex cells** fired for the same oriented edge *anywhere in a neighborhood* — the
same question, asked with tolerance for position. And the cells were organized hierarchically,
simple feeding complex, specific feeding tolerant. The work won the 1981 Nobel Prize, and it
handed neural-network researchers a blueprint: early vision is *local feature detectors, repeated
across space, followed by position-tolerant summaries*.

The first person to build that blueprint into a network was Kunihiko Fukushima. His
**Neocognitron** (1980) is the architectural ancestor of every CNN: alternating layers of
"S-cells" (simple — local feature detectors, the same detector replicated across the image) and
"C-cells" (complex — pooling those detections over a neighborhood), stacked into a hierarchy that
could recognize handwritten digits with tolerance to shifts and distortion. The anatomy of the
modern convolutional network is all there. What is missing is the learning. The Neocognitron was
trained layer by layer with a self-organizing, competitive scheme — no labels flowing back, no
end-to-end objective. It could not be *told* it was wrong about a digit and adjust its earliest
feature detectors accordingly. The body had arrived before the algorithm.

The algorithm, of course, is Module 01's: backpropagation. Yann LeCun — one of backprop's
mid-1980s rediscoverers — put the two together at Bell Labs. The 1989 paper *Backpropagation
Applied to Handwritten Zip Code Recognition* trained a small network of local, **shared-weight**
feature maps end-to-end on 16×16 images of digits cut from real U.S. Mail zip codes, and got
about a 5% test error rate — good enough that versions of it went on to read real mail. The
network (retroactively "LeNet-1") established the recipe this chapter implements: convolution
plus subsampling, stacked, finished with fully-connected layers, trained by plain gradient
descent from pixels to labels.

It also, unusually for the era, *shipped*. Bell Labs squeezed the trained network onto AT&T's
DSP-32C signal processor, recognizing digits in real time on early-1990s silicon, and versions
went into mail-sorting and form-reading products. A decade of that engineering — bigger training
sets, better normalization, an architecture revised through LeNet-4 into its final form — later,
*Gradient-Based Learning Applied to Document Recognition* (LeCun, Bottou, Bengio & Haffner,
1998) presented the polished version, LeNet-5, and, in its second, mostly forgotten half, the
full check-reading system built around it. The paper is a strange, wonderful document: half
architecture description, half systems engineering, written at the exact moment its subject was
both commercially triumphant and academically unfashionable.

That 1998 paper is also why our benchmark exists at all. To compare classifiers fairly, the
authors needed a clean, big, *honestly split* digit dataset. The government's NIST corpus had a
notorious flaw: its designated training set (SD-3) was written by neat-handed Census Bureau
employees and its test set (SD-1) by high-school students, so models looked far worse than they
were. LeCun and colleagues re-shuffled the two pools into a **M**odified NIST set — 60,000
training and 10,000 test images, size-normalized and centered into 28×28 — and MNIST has been the
fruit-fly of machine learning ever since — small enough to train in minutes today, hard enough
that progress on it meant something for two decades, and split honestly enough that the number
you report is the number you deserve. On it, LeNet-5 reached 0.95% error, and 0.8% with
distortion-augmented training — under one mistake per hundred digits, in 1998, on 1998 hardware.

|------|-----------|---------------|
| 1962 | Hubel & Wiesel | simple/complex cells: local detectors + position tolerance |
| 1980 | Fukushima, *Neocognitron* | the architecture: replicated local features, pooling, hierarchy |
| 1989 | LeCun, zip codes | backprop meets weight sharing — trained end-to-end, deployed on mail |
| 1998 | LeCun et al., LeNet-5 | the polished recipe, MNIST, and a system reading millions of checks |

## The intuition: stop shredding the picture

Here is the whole idea in one sentence: **an image is not a list of numbers, so stop feeding it
to the network as one — detect small patterns locally, and reuse the same detector everywhere.**

Think about what Module 01's MLP actually does to a digit. The `784`-pixel image is flattened
into a row vector, and every hidden unit gets its own private weight for every pixel. Nothing in
that architecture knows that pixel 155 sits directly above pixel 183. You could scramble all 784
pixels with a fixed permutation — turning every digit into confetti no human could read — and the
MLP would train to *exactly* the same accuracy, because to the MLP the image already is
confetti. All the structure that makes a "7" a 7 — two strokes, one horizontal, one diagonal,
meeting at a corner — is invisible to it as structure. It can memorize pixel correlations, given
enough weights, but it starts from nothing.

And "enough weights" is a lot of weights. Suppose we wanted a dense layer to do what this
chapter's first convolutional layer does: map the 28×28 input to six 24×24 grids of feature
detections. That is $784$ inputs to $6 \times 24 \times 24 = 3{,}456$ outputs — about **2.7
million weights** for one layer. Our conv layer does the same job with **156**. Where did the
other 2.7 million go? Two assumptions about images deleted them:

1. **Locality.** A pattern worth detecting — an edge, a stroke ending, a corner — lives in a
   small neighborhood. So let each output unit look at a 5×5 patch instead of the whole image:
   25 weights and a bias, not 784. Nothing three inches from a stroke changes what the stroke is.
2. **Weight sharing (translation).** A vertical-edge detector that is useful in the top-left
   corner is exactly as useful at every other position, because a stroke means the same thing
   wherever it falls. So don't learn 576 separate patch-detectors — learn **one** 5×5 detector
   and *slide it* to all 576 positions.

The slid detector is called a **kernel** (or filter), and its 576 answers, arranged back into
their 24×24 grid, are a **feature map**: a picture of *where the pattern is*. A kernel is a
question asked everywhere; the feature map is the sheet of answers, one per place. One question
is not enough — a "7" needs horizontal-ness *and* diagonal-ness *and* corners — so the layer
learns a small bank of kernels (six, in our first layer) and produces six feature maps, a
six-channel image of pattern-evidence.

Weight sharing does something subtler than saving memory, and it is worth pausing on. During
training, the gradient for the *one* shared kernel accumulates from *every* position in *every*
image — each patch it slid across is another lesson about what the kernel should be. The dense
layer's 2.7 million weights each learn from one pixel-position in however many images; the shared
25 learn from everything, everywhere. That is why convolutional nets need less data, not just
less memory: the architecture itself encodes the fact that appearance is translation-invariant,
so the network doesn't have to burn examples discovering it.

Now stack the idea, because this is where it stops being a trick and becomes a theory of vision.
The first layer's six feature maps are themselves a six-channel image — a picture whose "colors"
are *amounts of pattern-evidence*. So convolve **that**. A second-layer kernel spans all six
channels at once, which means it detects *arrangements of first-layer features*: "vertical-edge
evidence here, next to horizontal-edge evidence there" — a corner. A corner detector was never
programmed; it falls out of asking local questions about the answers to local questions. And
because each second-layer cell reads a 5×5 patch of maps that each summarize patches of the
original image, its effective window on the input — its **receptive field** — is much larger
than 5×5 (exercise 3 has you compute exactly how much). Edges compose into corners and stroke
junctions; in deeper networks those compose into textures, parts, and things. Hubel and Wiesel's
hierarchy, run to the top.

One more piece completes the intuition. After you know *where* the edges are to the nearest
pixel, you usually don't care to the nearest pixel — you care that there *is* a vertical stroke
on the right side, roughly. **Pooling** throws away the precision on purpose: summarize each 2×2
window of a feature map by its strongest response (max pooling), halving the resolution and
buying tolerance to small shifts. Hubel and Wiesel's division of labor, exactly: convolution
plays the simple cells, pooling plays the complex ones. Stack the pair twice — detect edges,
summarize; detect *arrangements of edges*, summarize — and finish with ordinary dense layers to
read out the class. That whole stack, on MNIST, is $44{,}426$ parameters. Module 01's MLP needed
$101{,}770$ to do a worse job.

## The math: correlation, convolution, and their gradients

Time to make it precise. We need a little new notation, consistent with everything so far: an
image batch is $X$ with shape $(n, C, H, W)$ — $n$ samples, $C$ channels, height $H$, width $W$
(so MNIST is $(n, 1, 28, 28)$). A kernel bank is $K$ with shape $(F, C, K_H, K_W)$ — $F$ filters,
each spanning all $C$ input channels — with one bias $b_f$ per filter. We write $u, v$ for a
position in the output map and $p, q$ for a position inside the kernel. ($K$ gets its letter
because $W$ is taken — it means image width here, and weight matrix everywhere else.)

### The operation

Lay the kernel on the image with its top-left corner at $(u, v)$, multiply elementwise, add
everything up, add the bias. That single number is one output cell; doing it at every valid
position is the whole operation:

$$
Y[i, f, u, v] \;=\; b_f \;+\; \sum_{c=0}^{C-1}\sum_{p=0}^{K_H-1}\sum_{q=0}^{K_W-1}
K[f, c, p, q]\; X[i, c, u+p,\, v+q].
$$

Because we require the kernel to fit entirely inside the image (**valid** placement, stride 1 —
all LeNet needs), the output shrinks: $u$ can range over

$$
H_{\text{out}} = H - K_H + 1, \qquad W_{\text{out}} = W - K_W + 1,
$$

so a 5×5 kernel turns 28×28 into 24×24.

Notice what the sum over $c$ does: a filter is not a 5×5 square but a $C \times K_H \times K_W$
**volume**, reading every channel of its patch at once and committing to a single number. Our
first layer's filters are $1 \times 5 \times 5$ (25 weights — grayscale in, one opinion out);
the second layer's are $6 \times 5 \times 5$ (150 weights), which is precisely how they get to
detect *combinations* of the first layer's features. Parameter count per layer is $F \cdot (C
\cdot K_H \cdot K_W + 1)$ — for our first layer, $6 \times 26 = 156$; for the second, $16 \times
(6 \cdot 25 + 1) = 2{,}416$.

### One cell, by hand

To keep the formula honest, run one cell of the bars toy on paper. Take a 3×3 vertical-edge
kernel and lay it on a patch whose middle column is a bright bar:

$$
K = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\text{patch} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}
\;\Rightarrow\; Y = 0.
$$

Zero! The kernel sees the bar's *body*, where left and right neighbors match, and correctly
reports "no edge here". Slide one column left, so the kernel's $+1$ column sits on the bar:
now $Y = 3$ — a strong positive "bright-on-my-right" edge. One more column left and $Y = 0$
again; approach from the other side and the $-1$ column catches the bar, $Y = -3$. The feature
map of a vertical bar is therefore a positive stripe and a negative stripe hugging the bar's two
edges — which is exactly the signature you will see in the widget below, and exactly why the
sign-carrying diverging colormap matters. The number is not "bar-ness"; it is *signed evidence
of a specific local transition*.

### Strides and padding, for the road ahead

Two dials we set to their simplest values deserve their names now, because the next modules turn
them. We slide the kernel one pixel at a time; sliding $S$ pixels at a time — a **stride** of
$S$ — computes every $S$-th answer and shrinks the output by roughly that factor (AlexNet opens
with a stride-4 layer for exactly that economy). And we required the kernel to fit inside the
image; **padding** the border with $P_{\text{pad}}$ rings of zeros lets windows center on edge
pixels and can keep the output the same size as the input ("same" padding). The general formula,
worth deriving once from a number line and then trusting forever:

$$
H_{\text{out}} = \left\lfloor \frac{H + 2P_{\text{pad}} - K_H}{S} \right\rfloor + 1.
$$

With $S = 1, P_{\text{pad}} = 0$ it collapses to our $H - K_H + 1$. LeNet-5 itself made one
padding move — feeding 28×28 digits in as 32×32 — for reasons the historical section will
honor.

One honest confession about the name. What the formula above computes — kernel slid without
flipping — is what a mathematician calls **cross-correlation**. A true *convolution* flips the
kernel in both axes first ($K[p,q] \to K[K_H{-}1{-}p,\, K_W{-}1{-}q]$), a convention that makes
convolution commutative and Fourier-friendly. For learning it makes no difference whatsoever:
the network would simply learn the flipped kernel. So, like PyTorch, TensorFlow, and every other
modern framework, we compute cross-correlation and call the layer `conv2d` anyway. Keep the
distinction in your pocket, though — the flip is about to sneak back in on its own.

Pooling is simpler. Non-overlapping $P \times P$ max pooling (we use $P = 2$) partitions each
feature map into little windows and keeps each window's maximum:

$$
Y[i, c, u, v] \;=\; \max_{0 \le p, q < P} \; X[i, c,\, uP{+}p,\, vP{+}q],
$$

halving both dimensions. Average pooling — LeNet-5's original choice — takes the mean instead.

### The backward pass, all three gradients

The forward formula is a big sum of products, and every product involves one kernel weight and
one pixel. So the chain rule has three jobs: route each output-cell gradient $dY[i,f,u,v] =
\partial L / \partial Y[i,f,u,v]$ back to the bias, to the kernel weights, and to the pixels.
As always in this book, gradients are summed over the batch and the $1/m$ lives in the loss.

**The bias** appears once in every output cell of its filter, with coefficient 1, so its
gradient is just the sum of everything downstream:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial b_f} = \sum_{i,u,v} dY[i, f, u, v].
$$

**The kernel.** The weight $K[f,c,p,q]$ multiplied pixel $X[i,c,u+p,v+q]$ in the output cell
$(u,v)$ — once per output cell. Chain rule: multiply each cell's gradient by the pixel that
weight touched there, and add up:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial K[f, c, p, q]} = \sum_{i,u,v} dY[i, f, u, v]\; X[i, c, u+p,\, v+q].
$$

Look at the shape of that formula: it slides an index over the image and sums products — it *is*
the forward operation, with the upstream gradient playing the role of the kernel. **The gradient
of a correlation is another correlation**, between the input and the incoming gradient. The
operation teaches itself.

**The input.** Pixel $X[i,c,h,w]$ contributed to output cell $(u, v)$ through kernel tap
$(p, q)$ exactly when $h = u + p$ and $w = v + q$. To collect its gradient, sum over all the
output cells it fed — substitute $u = h - p$, $v = w - q$:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial X[i, c, h, w]} = \sum_{f}\sum_{p,q}
dY[i, f, h - p,\, w - q]\; K[f, c, p, q],
$$

with terms where $(h-p,\, w-q)$ falls outside the output simply absent. Stare at the indices:
as $p$ and $q$ *increase*, the position we read from $dY$ *decreases*. That is a correlation
with the kernel **flipped in both axes** — a true convolution, in the mathematician's sense —
of the (zero-padded) upstream gradient with $K$. We chose not to flip in the forward pass, and
the flip reappeared, uninvited, in the backward pass. It was never optional; it just moves.

**Pooling** has no parameters, so it only routes. Max pooling's forward remembered, for each
window, *where* the maximum was; its backward sends the whole incoming gradient to that winning
cell and zero everywhere else — the same on/off gate as ReLU's mask, and the second time this
book's answer to "how do you differentiate a choice?" is *remember the choice, route the
gradient through it*. (At an exact tie the max isn't differentiable, but ties between continuous
values are a measure-zero event; every implementation just picks the first winner.) Average
pooling spreads the gradient evenly: each input cell in a window receives $dY/P^2$.

One last piece of arithmetic joins the toolkit. He initialization (Module 02) scales weight
draws by $\sqrt{2/\text{fan-in}}$, and a conv output unit's fan-in is everything one dot product
reads: $C \cdot K_H \cdot K_W$ — 25 for a single-channel 5×5, 150 for our second layer. Same
rule, new count.

## The code: the same chain rule, now with four more loops

Everything above is maybe twenty lines of code, written straight from the formulas. The forward
pass in C ([`lib/c/nanograd/conv.c`](https://github.com/shaia/deep-learning-from-scratch/blob/main/lib/c/nanograd/conv.c)
— convolution and pooling join the shared library this module, alongside Module 02's layers):

```c
/* Y[i,f,u,v] = b[f] + sum_{c,p,q} K[f,c,p,q] * X[i,c,u+p,v+q] */
for (int i = 0; i < n; i++)
    for (int f = 0; f < F; f++)
        for (int u = 0; u < OH; u++)
            for (int v = 0; v < OW; v++) {
                double acc = b[f];
                for (int c = 0; c < C; c++)
                    for (int p = 0; p < KH; p++)
                        for (int q = 0; q < KW; q++)
                            acc += K[((f * C + c) * KH + p) * KW + q]
                                 * X[((i * C + c) * H + (u + p)) * W + (v + q)];
                Y[((i * F + f) * OH + u) * OW + v] = acc;
            }
```

Seven nested loops, and every one of them earns its place: batch, filter, two output
coordinates, then channel and two kernel taps for the dot product. The Python reference
(`naive_conv2d_forward` in
[`python/lenet.py`](https://github.com/shaia/deep-learning-from-scratch/blob/main/topics/03-lenet/python/lenet.py))
is the same seven loops with NumPy indexing, kept as the module's ground truth.

The backward pass reuses the *same* loop nest — walk the output cells again, and this time push
each cell's gradient `g` back into the bias, the kernel, and (when a layer sits below) the
input:

```c
double g = dY[((i * F + f) * OH + u) * OW + v];
if (g == 0.0) continue;        /* ReLU and pooling leave most cells dead */
db[f] += g;
for (int c = 0; c < C; c++)
    for (int p = 0; p < KH; p++)
        for (int q = 0; q < KW; q++) {
            dK[...] += g * X[((i * C + c) * H + (u + p)) * W + (v + q)];
            if (dX) dX[((i * C + c) * H + (u + p)) * W + (v + q)]
                        += g * K[...];
        }
```

No flipped-kernel gymnastics in sight — writing the backward as "for each output, scatter to
everything that produced it" implements the flipped convolution *implicitly*, because the
indices `u+p`, `v+q` do the substitution for us. The formula and the loop are the same fact in
two notations.

Max pooling's forward stores each window winner's flat offset in an `idx` array; its backward is
then two lines — zero the buffer, scatter:

```c
for (int j = 0; j < n * C * H * W; j++) dX[j] = 0.0;
for (int o = 0; o < n * C * OH * OW; o++)
    dX[idx[o]] += dY[o];       /* winner takes the whole gradient */
```

And here is this chapter's favorite C fact. After the second pooling layer we must "flatten" the
$(n, 16, 4, 4)$ feature maps into $(n, 256)$ rows for the dense head — and in C **the flatten
does not exist**. Our tensors are flat row-major `NCHW` buffers, `X[((i*C + c)*H + h)*W + w]`,
which means sample $i$'s 256 feature values already sit contiguously, one sample per row:
*exactly* the $(m, n_\text{in})$ matrix layout `ng_linear_forward` has expected since Module 02.
In [`c/lenet.c`](https://github.com/shaia/deep-learning-from-scratch/blob/main/topics/03-lenet/c/lenet.c)
the pooled buffer is simply passed to the linear layer, and a comment marks the spot where
nothing happens. The Python `Flatten` layer is a `reshape` — also free — existing mostly so the
`Sequential` stack can run uniformly and restore shapes on the way back.

### The toy program, end to end

With the ops in the library,
[`c/lenet.c`](https://github.com/shaia/deep-learning-from-scratch/blob/main/topics/03-lenet/c/lenet.c)
reads like a table of contents for the whole chapter. Two independent RNG streams (data and
weights, so neither can perturb the other), fixed-size buffers for every activation and
gradient, and then a training step that is just the forward chain and its mirror image:

```c
/* forward: conv -> ReLU -> maxpool -> (flatten: free) -> linear -> loss */
ng_conv2d_forward(X, K1, b1, Z, N, 1, 8, 8, 4, 3, 3);
ng_relu_forward(Z, A, N * 4 * 6 * 6);
ng_maxpool2d_forward(A, POOLED, IDX, N, 4, 6, 6, 2);
ng_linear_forward(POOLED, W2, b2, LOGITS, N, 36, 2);
double loss = ng_softmax_ce_forward(LOGITS, y, P, N, 2);

/* backward: the reversed walk */
ng_softmax_ce_backward(P, y, DLOGITS, N, 2);
ng_linear_backward(POOLED, W2, DLOGITS, DPOOL, gW2, gb2, N, 36, 2);
ng_maxpool2d_backward(IDX, DPOOL, DA, N, 4, 6, 6, 2);
ng_relu_backward(Z, DA, DZ, N * 4 * 6 * 6);
ng_conv2d_backward(X, K1, DZ, NULL, gK1, gb1, N, 1, 8, 8, 4, 3, 3);
```

Backpropagation through a CNN is *still* the reversed walk it was in Module 01 — pooling and
convolution slot into the chain like any other layer, each consuming the gradient the layer
above produced. Note the `NULL`: the first conv touches the raw data, so nobody needs $dX$
there, and the library skips computing it. Adam then updates all four parameter arrays, with the
bias-correction powers advanced by multiplication rather than `pow()` — the Module-02 trick that
keeps C and Python from disagreeing about a last bit.

The Python mirror in `python/lenet.py` deliberately contains **two** implementations. `ToyCNN`
is a scalar, flat-array, loop-for-loop twin of the C — the agreement gate — while
`build_toy_cnn()` assembles the identical network from the vectorized `nanograd` layers
(`Conv2D`, `ReLU`, `MaxPool2D`, `Flatten`, `Linear`) drawing the same He init from the same
seed. A third test gate pins the two Python paths to each other, so the readable version and
the fast version can never silently drift apart.

The naive loops are the definition, and in C they are also fast enough. In Python they are not —
NumPy pays an interpreter toll per index — and this is where the one genuinely clever trick of
the chapter comes in: **im2col**. Copy every 5×5 patch out as a *row* of one big matrix (a
`(n·24·24, 25)` matrix for our first layer), reshape the kernel bank to `(6, 25)`, and the
entire convolution — all filters, all positions, all images — collapses into a single matrix
multiplication:

```python
def forward(self, X):
    self.cols = im2col(X, KH, KW)          # (n*OH*OW, C*KH*KW): one patch per row
    Kmat = self.K.reshape(F, C * KH * KW)  # one filter per row
    out = self.cols @ Kmat.T + self.b      # ONE matmul = the whole convolution
    return out.reshape(n, OH, OW, F).transpose(0, 3, 1, 2)
```

Interior pixels get copied into 25 different rows — im2col trades memory redundancy for the one
operation computers do at full speed. In the notebook the two paths agree to `1.3e-15` and the
matmul version is **about 270× faster**; the backward pass rides the same trick (two matmuls,
then the adjoint `col2im` scatter-adds overlapping patch gradients back — which is precisely
$dX$). This is not just our trick: under every framework's `conv2d` lives some descendant of
im2col or its Fourier and Winograd cousins. Convolution, in practice, *is* matrix
multiplication wearing a costume.

## The gradient check, and C↔Python agreement

Module 03 adds the most index-heavy code in the book so far — seven-deep loops are exactly where
off-by-one bugs go to hide. The verification gates stay merciless about it.

First, honesty: every hand-written backward is checked against centered finite differences.
Nudge one weight by $h$, run the forward twice, difference the losses — if the analytic gradient
is right, the two agree to about `1e-8`, and
[`tests/test_agreement.py`](https://github.com/shaia/deep-learning-from-scratch/blob/main/topics/03-lenet/tests/test_agreement.py)
demands it for `Conv2D`'s $dK$, $db$, and $dX$, both poolings, and the whole toy CNN
end-to-end through softmax cross-entropy. (Max pooling gets continuous random inputs so its
measure-zero kinks stay unvisited.) The im2col fast path must additionally match the naive
seven-loop definition at `1e-12` — same arithmetic, different order.

Second, agreement: the C and Python implementations train the same tiny CNN on the same toy data
(next section) from the same RNG streams, in the same operation order — the loop nests above are
a *contract*, not a style choice — and must land on the same trained network. After 300 Adam
steps, each side prints a six-value fingerprint at full `%.17g` precision: final loss, accuracy,
`wsum` (a checksum summing all 114 parameters in a fixed order, so *any* divergence anywhere
poisons it), and three sentinel weights. The test parses C's `FINAL` line, runs the Python
mirror, and compares. The gate is `1e-9` relative; the observed drift is around `1e-15` — the
last-ulp residue of `exp` and `sqrt` differing across math libraries, after three hundred
steps of convolution, pooling's argmax routing, and Adam's moment arithmetic agreeing about
everything else. The scalar-mirror treatment is reserved for the toy: MNIST runs only on the
vectorized path and is checked by metric, because bit-tracking a 44,426-parameter training run
buys no additional honesty that the toy hasn't already bought.

## Toy → real: bars, then 98.5% on MNIST

As always, the mechanism proves itself on a toy first. Ours is **bars**: sixteen noisy 8×8
images, each containing one full-brightness bar — vertical (class 0) or horizontal (class 1) —
at a random position. It is the smallest problem where locality genuinely pays: position is
random, so any fixed-position detector (an MLP's only kind) must memorize all the positions,
while a single oriented 3×3 kernel, slid everywhere, solves the problem *structurally*. A
four-filter CNN with 114 parameters — `conv 4@3×3 → ReLU → maxpool → linear` — hits 100%
within thirty Adam steps, in both C and NumPy, matching to ~`1e-15`. And then the payoff of a
model small enough to look at: plot the four trained 3×3 kernels, and they *are* oriented edge
detectors — strong vertical and horizontal structure, invented from gradients alone, because
that is the only question on this data worth asking everywhere.

Then the real thing. The full network is LeNet-5's silhouette with Module 02's toolkit inside:

```
conv 6@5×5 → ReLU → maxpool 2×2      28×28 → 6@24×24 → 6@12×12       156 params
conv 16@5×5 → ReLU → maxpool 2×2     → 16@8×8 → 16@4×4             2,416
flatten → 120 → ReLU → 84 → ReLU → 10 logits              30,840 + 10,164 + 850
                                                          total: 44,426
```

He init, Adam at `1e-3`, batch 128, the full 60,000 training images, **three epochs** — a few
minutes of pure NumPy on a laptop:

| epoch | train loss | test accuracy |
|-------|-----------:|--------------:|
| 0 | 0.260 | 97.00% |
| 1 | 0.086 | 98.30% |
| 2 | 0.062 | **98.46%** |

The verification gate asserts ≥ 98%, and the run clears it with margin — **154 of 10,000** test
digits missed. Module 01's MLP, trained far longer, reached 97% with $101{,}770$ parameters; the
CNN beats it by half the errors using **fewer than half the weights**, because those weights are
spent on assumptions that are actually true of images. (The 1998 paper's full recipe, trained
for twenty passes with its own tricks, reached 0.95% error; our three NumPy epochs are chasing
the mechanism, not the leaderboard.)

The notebook then does the thing this chapter has been promising: it *looks inside*. Conv1's six
trained 5×5 kernels, plotted as signed heatmaps, are oriented edge and stroke detectors — the
toy's four kernels again, discovered independently at MNIST scale, and Hubel and Wiesel's simple
cells a third time, discovered by gradient descent. (These same six kernels are the "learned
#1–#6" presets in the widget above — exported from this training run, not redrawn.) Conv2's 150-
weight volumes resist flat plotting — they live in six-channel feature space, not pixel space —
which is itself the lesson: interpretability gets harder exactly as representations get more
abstract. And the misclassified gallery parades the 154 failures, mostly digits a human would
also squint at: gaunt 9s mistaken for 4s, 7s crossed like 2s.

For honesty across the aisle, the
[notebook](https://github.com/shaia/deep-learning-from-scratch/blob/main/topics/03-lenet/notebook.ipynb)
closes by rebuilding everything in the frameworks. PyTorch first, strictly: `F.conv2d` and
`F.max_pool2d` are handed our exact kernels in float64 and the same upstream gradients, and
forward outputs, $dK$, $db$, $dX$, and every parameter gradient of the full toy CNN match our
hand-written versions to ~`1e-15` — there is one right answer and both implementations sit on
it. Then TensorFlow (a `GradientTape` LeNet: **98.69%**) and Keras (the same network in nine
lines: **98.63%**) confirm the architecture reaches the same place, at the same cost, whoever
implements it.

## What LeNet-5 actually was

What we built is LeNet-5's *shape* filled with 2020s components. The honest thing is to lay the
1998 original beside it, because every difference is a lesson from Module 02 applied backward
in time — and because the original's choices were not mistakes, but correct answers to 1998's
constraints.

Walk the real LeNet-5 from the paper. Input is not 28×28 but **32×32** — MNIST digits are padded
with a border so that a first-layer receptive field can *center* on strokes at the image's edge
rather than half-fall off it (with valid 5×5 convolutions, only pixels ≥ 2 from the border get
to be a window's center). Then C1: 6 feature maps of 5×5 convolutions, just like ours. Then S2 —
and here the differences start. LeNet-5's "subsampling" is average pooling *with learnable
knobs*: each map's 2×2 average is multiplied by a trained coefficient, given a trained bias,
and passed through a sigmoid — pooling as a tiny neuron. Modern practice (and our build) found
the knobs unnecessary: a plain max does the summarizing job with zero parameters and a friendlier
gradient (a clean route, rather than a sigmoid's squash).

Between S2 and C3 sits the original's most curious artifact: the **connection table**. Our conv2
reads all six input channels with every filter. LeNet-5's sixteen C3 maps each read only a
hand-chosen *subset* — the first six read three adjacent maps, the next nine read four, one
reads all six — a sparse wiring diagram printed as Table 1 of the paper. The stated reasons: it
cut multiplications (1998 mattered), and it *broke symmetry* — different filters see different
inputs, so they are pushed toward learning different features. Modern nets dropped the table
(dense connectivity plus random init breaks symmetry fine, and the compute stopped mattering),
which makes it easy to forget the table was also an idea with descendants — grouped convolutions,
of which AlexNet's two-GPU split and today's depthwise-separable layers are the direct family
line.

The activations are **scaled tanh**: $f(a) = 1.7159 \tanh(\tfrac{2}{3} a)$, constants chosen so
that $f(\pm 1) = \pm 1$ — hand-tuning the function so that unit-variance signals stay
unit-variance through depth. Read that twice: it is *initialization-era variance control, done
inside the activation*, a decade before Xavier and He wrote down the general rule. The weight
init, uniform in $\pm 2.4 / \text{fan-in}$, is a Xavier ancestor by the same logic. Even the
optimizer rhymes: the paper trains with a *stochastic diagonal Levenberg–Marquardt* method that
maintains a **per-parameter learning rate** from second-derivative estimates — squint, and Adam
is the modern answer to exactly the itch it was scratching.

The output layer is the strangest part to modern eyes: not softmax but **RBF units**, one per
class, each scoring the 84-dimensional F6 vector by its squared distance to a fixed prototype —
and the prototypes are stylized 7×12 *bitmaps of the characters themselves*. Distance small,
class likely. It was built that way for the sake of the surrounding system (rejecting
non-digits, sharing a printable-character alphabet); softmax cross-entropy (our Module 01
machinery) replaced it everywhere because it trains better and needs no hand-drawn prototypes.

And that surrounding system is the paper's forgotten half. *Gradient-Based Learning Applied to
Document Recognition* spends its last thirty pages on **graph transformer networks** — a scheme
for training the entire check-reading pipeline (field location, segmentation into candidate
characters, recognition, and a language-model-ish interpretation of amounts) as *one*
differentiable system, gradients flowing through the graph of hypotheses end to end. The
deployed NCR system was that whole pipeline. If "train the entire system end-to-end with
gradients, not just the recognizer" sounds like the thesis of modern deep learning — it is, and
this is the paper that shipped it to a bank near you in 1998.

Step back from the parts list and the pattern is hard to miss: nearly every "odd" choice in
LeNet-5 is a hand-crafted, special-case solution to a problem the field later solved *in
general*. The scaled tanh hand-balances variance; Xavier and He derive the balance from fan-in.
The diagonal Levenberg–Marquardt optimizer hand-estimates per-parameter step sizes; Adam
estimates them from running moments, for any architecture. The C3 table hand-rations compute;
hardware growth made the ration pointless. This is what progress in deep learning has mostly
looked like — not new dreams, but *general* solutions replacing bespoke ones, so that the same
recipe works without a resident genius tuning it.

Our modernizations, then, in one honest table: ReLU where tanh saturated (Module 02's activation
lesson), max pooling where trained averages added knobs without value, He init where $2.4/F$
guessed, Adam where diagonal Levenberg–Marquardt labored, softmax where RBF prototypes posed,
full connectivity where the C3 table rationed multiplications. The notebook runs the closest
in-library approximation of the 1998 recipe (tanh, average pooling, Xavier) head-to-head against
our build on a 10k-image budget: **92.0% versus 94.3%** after two epochs. The old machine works
— it just climbs slower, exactly as Module 02 predicts.

## What it couldn't do

If convolution was this good in 1998 — deployed, profitable, under 1% error — why did computer
vision spend the next decade mostly ignoring it?

Because outside of digits, the recipe starved. Scale the images from 28×28 to real photographs
and the compute explodes — and 1998 had CPUs measured in hundreds of megahertz, so training
LeNet-5 took days; a photo-scale network was out of the question. Scale the *classes* from 10
digits to the actual visual world and the data requirement explodes too — and nothing like
ImageNet existed. Meanwhile the machine-learning mainstream had found support vector machines:
convex, elegant, theoretically clean, and on the small datasets of the era, just as accurate as
a CNN with none of the dark art. The 1998 paper itself shows SVMs breathing down LeNet's neck on
MNIST. Grad students follow the gradient; the field's flowed away from neural networks, and
convolutional nets spent the 2000s as a niche kept alive by a stubborn few — LeCun among them —
waiting for the two exponentials that would change the answer.

The in-between years were not empty — they were *narrow*. CNNs kept winning wherever the data
was industrial and the domain was bounded: check and form reading, an early face-detection
system, document analysis. LeCun kept building them at NYU; a small community kept the craft
alive on datasets like NORB and Caltech-101, forever a few points shy of convincing anyone. The
2006 "deep learning" revival — Hinton's deep belief networks, the rebranding of layer-stacking
as a research program — rewarmed the climate, and by the late 2000s the first GPU
implementations of CNNs were posting suspiciously good numbers on small benchmarks. Everything
was in place except the demonstration nobody could argue with.

Both missing exponentials arrived by 2012. Video games had spent the 2000s subsidizing massively
parallel processors
— GPUs — that happened to be shaped exactly like a convolution. And the web plus Amazon
Mechanical Turk produced ImageNet: 1.2 million labeled photographs, a thousand classes. Put
LeNet's recipe on that fuel, scale it up, and add a few of Module 02's tricks at scale — that is
**AlexNet**, the 2012 result that ended the argument and started the era this book spends the
rest of its chapters in. Same convolutions. Same weight sharing. Same backprop. Just three
orders of magnitude more of everything.
