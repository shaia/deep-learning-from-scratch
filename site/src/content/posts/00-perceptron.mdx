## Prologue: can a machine learn a decision?

Every network in this book — every convolutional filter, every attention head, every
billion-parameter language model — is a descendant of a single idea that fits on an index card:
add up your inputs, and fire if the total crosses a line. The story of deep learning is really
the story of what happens when you stack that idea, learn it from data, and let it grow. So we
start where the field started, with the humblest version of it, and build up the intuition,
the mathematics, and the running code by hand.

### 1943: a neuron as arithmetic

In 1943, the neurophysiologist Warren McCulloch and the logician Walter Pitts asked a question
that sounds almost reckless: could you describe a *neuron* — a living brain cell — with nothing
but arithmetic and logic? Their paper, *A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous
Activity*, answered yes. They modelled a neuron as an **all-or-none** unit: it receives inputs,
adds them up, and either fires or stays silent depending on whether the sum clears a fixed
**threshold**. Wire enough of these units together, they showed, and you can compute any
proposition in logic — a network of idealised neurons is, in principle, a logic circuit.

It was a breathtaking abstraction, and it is the foundation everything else rests on. But it had
one fatal limitation for our purposes: a McCulloch–Pitts network *cannot learn*. Every
connection strength and every threshold is fixed by hand by the designer. The machine can
compute, but it cannot improve from experience.

### 1957: a machine that tunes itself

The leap came in 1957, when the psychologist Frank Rosenblatt built the **perceptron** at the
Cornell Aeronautical Laboratory. The unit was the same — a weighted sum through a threshold —
but Rosenblatt added the missing ingredient: a **learning rule**. Show the perceptron labelled
examples, and a simple, mechanical procedure would adjust the weights until it classified them
correctly. The weights were no longer chosen by a human; they were *found* from data.

The first perceptron was not a program but a machine. The **Mark I Perceptron** sensed the world
through a 20×20 grid of 400 photocells; its weights were physical **potentiometers**, and small
electric **motors** turned them during learning. When the U.S. Navy demonstrated it in 1958, the
press lost its composure. The *New York Times* reported on "the embryo of an electronic computer
that [the Navy] expects will be able to walk, talk, see, write, reproduce itself and be
conscious of its existence." The reality was more modest — and its limits, which we will reach
and prove later in this chapter, would stall the whole field for years.

Here is the arc this chapter follows:

| Year | Milestone | What it added |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| 1943 | McCulloch & Pitts | the neuron *as arithmetic* — but fixed by hand |
| 1957–58 | Rosenblatt's perceptron | weights *learned from examples* |
| 1969 | Minsky & Papert | a *proof* of the single-layer limit (XOR) |
| 1986 | Backpropagation | learning *stacked* layers — the escape (Module 01) |

## From a brain cell to arithmetic

Strip a biological neuron to its computational cartoon and three parts remain. The **dendrites**
carry incoming signals, each arriving over a connection of some strength. The **soma** (cell
body) sums those signals. And the **axon** fires an all-or-none spike — a fixed pulse, not a
graded one — when the accumulated input is large enough. The neuron does not report *how much*
the threshold was exceeded; it reports only *whether* it was.

The artificial version keeps exactly this shape. Give it an input vector $x = (x_0, x_1)$. Attach
a **weight** $w_j$ to each input — the artificial analogue of a connection strength, saying *how
much that input matters and in which direction*. Sum the weighted inputs, add a **bias** $b$ that
shifts the threshold, and fire when the total is at least zero:

$$
z = w_0 x_0 + w_1 x_1 + b, \qquad
\hat{y} = \begin{cases} 1 & z \ge 0 \\ 0 & z < 0. \end{cases}
$$

We call $z$ the **pre-activation** and $\hat{y}$ the prediction. The threshold test $z \ge 0$ is
the **step** (or Heaviside) function — the mathematical stand-in for all-or-none firing. That is
the entire McCulloch–Pitts neuron.

### One neuron is already three logic gates

McCulloch and Pitts' claim was that such units compute logic. It is worth seeing this concretely,
because it is where the perceptron's power *and* its limit both come from. Choose the weights by
hand and a single unit becomes a logic gate:

- **AND** fires only when *both* inputs are 1. Take $w = (1, 1)$, $b = -1.5$: the sum $x_0 + x_1$
  reaches $1.5$ only at $(1,1)$.
- **OR** fires when *either* input is 1. Take $w = (1, 1)$, $b = -0.5$: any single 1 already
  clears the threshold.
- **NOT** flips its one input. Take $w = (-1, 0)$, $b = 0.5$: input $0$ fires, input $1$ does not.

**single straight line** — and read off the exact weights and bias each gate uses:

This is the crux. A single perceptron can compute any function whose "fires" and "doesn't-fire"
cases can be separated by one straight line. AND, OR, and NOT all qualify. Hold on to the word
*single* — one line — because the whole drama of this chapter is the function for which one line
is not enough.

The catch, again, is that we *chose* those weights. McCulloch and Pitts would have set the
potentiometers by hand. Rosenblatt's question was: could the machine find them itself?

## The intuition: one line that moves

Geometrically, the boundary between "fires" and "stays silent" is the set of points where
$z = 0$ — a straight line in the plane (a *hyperplane* in higher dimensions). On one side the
perceptron says class 1; on the other, class 0. **Learning is nothing more than moving that
line.**

The rule is intuitive before it is formal. Feed the perceptron an example. If it gets the answer
right, leave the weights alone. If it gets the answer wrong, give the line a small shove in the
direction that would have fixed the mistake. Repeat. If a line that separates your two classes
exists at all, this shoving is *guaranteed* to find one — a promise we will actually prove.

Play with it. Press **Play** and watch the boundary swing into place on two blobs of data; each
update nudges the line toward a point it currently gets wrong (ringed in pink):

Then switch to **XOR** and press Play again. No matter how long it runs, the line never settles —
it sweeps back and forth and separates nothing. That failure is not a bug, and it is not a matter
of training longer. It is the wall this chapter is named for. First, though, let us understand
exactly what those weights *mean*.

## The geometry: what the weights mean

The algebra $z = w \cdot x + b$ hides a clean geometric picture, and seeing it makes the learning
rule obvious rather than magical. Three facts:

1. **The weight vector $w$ is the boundary's normal.** The line $z = 0$ runs *perpendicular* to
   $w$. Rotating $w$ rotates the boundary with it, always at a right angle.
2. **The bias $b$ slides the boundary.** With $b = 0$ the line passes through the origin; making
   $b$ more negative pushes the line in the direction $w$ points, without rotating it.
3. **The pre-activation $z$ measures signed distance.** For any point $x$, the quantity
   $z / \lVert w \rVert$ is exactly the signed perpendicular distance from $x$ to the boundary —
   positive on the "fires" side, negative on the other, and larger in magnitude the farther away
   the point is. So $z$ is not just a yes/no vote; it is a graded measure of *how confidently*,
   and it is that gradient the learning rule exploits.

Drag the $w$ arrow and the probe point to see all three at once — the line stays perpendicular to
$w$, the dashed segment is the shortest path to the boundary, and its signed length is
$z / \lVert w \rVert$:

<BoundaryGeometryWidget />

With this picture in hand, Rosenblatt's rule stops being a formula to memorise. A misclassified
point sits on the wrong side of the line; to fix it we must move the line *toward* it, which
means nudging the normal $w$ in the direction of that point. That is precisely what the rule does.

## The math (all of it)

We now have every piece. The model is the linear unit and its step:

$$
z = w_0 x_0 + w_1 x_1 + b, \qquad
\hat{y} = \begin{cases} 1 & z \ge 0 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
$$

Learning is **Rosenblatt's rule**. For each example, look at the error $\,y - \hat{y}\,$, which is
$-1$, $0$, or $+1$, and nudge the weights and bias:

$$
w \leftarrow w + \eta\,(y - \hat{y})\,x, \qquad b \leftarrow b + \eta\,(y - \hat{y}),
$$

where $\eta$ (the **learning rate**, the `lr` slider in the widget above) sets the step size. If
the prediction is right, $y - \hat{y} = 0$ and nothing moves. That is the whole algorithm — no
calculus, no autograd, no solver.

### Why the update works

It is worth proving to yourself that the rule always pushes in the *right* direction. Suppose the
perceptron misclassifies a point $x$, so $y - \hat{y} \ne 0$. Apply the update and ask what
happens to the pre-activation *at that same point*. The new weights give

$$
z' = w' \cdot x + b' = z + \eta\,(y - \hat{y})\,(\lVert x \rVert^2 + 1).
$$

The factor $\lVert x \rVert^2 + 1$ is always positive, so $z$ moves in the sign of the error
$(y - \hat{y})$. If the true label is $1$ but the unit stayed silent ($\hat{y} = 0$, error
$+1$), then $z$ was negative and the update *increases* it — toward firing. If the true label is
$0$ but the unit fired ($\hat{y} = 1$, error $-1$), the update *decreases* $z$ — toward silence.
Every correction moves the point's score toward the correct side of the line. That is the local
guarantee; the global one has a name.

### The Perceptron Convergence Theorem

Rosenblatt's rule does not merely improve — on separable data it *finishes*, in a bounded number
of steps, no matter how the weights start. This is the **Perceptron Convergence Theorem**
(Novikoff, 1962). State it with $\pm 1$ labels and the bias folded into $w$ (append a constant
$1$ to every $x$), so a mistake-driven update is $w \leftarrow w + y\,x$:

> Suppose the data is linearly separable — there is a unit vector $w^\*$ and a **margin**
> $\gamma > 0$ with $y_n (w^\* \cdot x_n) \ge \gamma$ for every example — and every input satisfies
> $\lVert x_n \rVert \le R$. Then, starting from $w = 0$, the perceptron makes at most
> $\left(R/\gamma\right)^2$ mistakes before separating the data.

The proof is two inequalities that squeeze the number of mistakes $k$ from both sides. Each update
happens only on a mistake, on some point $(x, y)$.

**Lower bound — the weights line up with $w^\*$.** Dotting the update with $w^\*$,

$$
w^\* \cdot w_{k} = w^\* \cdot w_{k-1} + y\,(w^\* \cdot x) \ge w^\* \cdot w_{k-1} + \gamma,
$$

so after $k$ mistakes $w^\* \cdot w_k \ge k\gamma$.

**Upper bound — the weights cannot grow too fast.** Because the point was misclassified,
$y\,(w_{k-1} \cdot x) < 0$, so

$$
\lVert w_k \rVert^2 = \lVert w_{k-1} \rVert^2 + 2\,y\,(w_{k-1} \cdot x) + \lVert x \rVert^2
\le \lVert w_{k-1} \rVert^2 + R^2,
$$

giving $\lVert w_k \rVert^2 \le k R^2$.

**Squeeze.** Since $w^\*$ is a unit vector, Cauchy–Schwarz gives
$w^\* \cdot w_k \le \lVert w_k \rVert$. Chaining the two bounds,

$$
k\gamma \;\le\; w^\* \cdot w_k \;\le\; \lVert w_k \rVert \;\le\; \sqrt{k}\,R
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad k \le \left(\frac{R}{\gamma}\right)^{2}.
$$

The mistake count is finite and — strikingly — *independent of the dimension* and of how many
examples you have. It depends only on the geometry: the reach of the data $R$ against the margin
$\gamma$. A wider gap between the classes means faster convergence. (Our 0/1 code uses the same
mistake-driven flavour; the $\pm 1$ form just makes the algebra clean.)

## The code: from scratch, and bit-for-bit

The whole project keeps a strict contract (pedagogy principle #3): the **C and Python
implementations produce identical numbers on the same seed**, so a test can hold them to each
other to $10^{-9}$ rather than "roughly agrees." That contract shapes even the boring parts of
the code, so it is worth seeing more than just the update.

### A deterministic RNG we can reproduce in two languages

NumPy's random generator would make the two languages diverge immediately, so we roll our own —
a 64-bit **linear congruential generator** with the well-known Knuth/PCG constants — and use only
integer arithmetic, which is exact in both languages. In C:

```c
typedef struct { uint64_t state; } Rng;

static uint64_t rng_next_u64(Rng *r) {
    r->state = r->state * 6364136223846793005ULL + 1442695040888963407ULL;
    return r->state;
}
static double rng_uniform(Rng *r) {          /* top 53 bits / 2^53  -> [0,1) */
    return (double)(rng_next_u64(r) >> 11) * (1.0 / 9007199254740992.0);
}
```

The Python mirror emulates 64-bit unsigned overflow with an explicit mask so the *bit stream*
matches exactly:

```python
_MASK64 = (1 << 64) - 1

class Rng:
    def __init__(self, seed): self.state = seed & _MASK64
    def next_u64(self):
        self.state = (self.state * 6364136223846793005 + 1442695040888963407) & _MASK64
        return self.state
    def uniform(self):                        # top 53 bits / 2^53 -> [0,1)
        return (self.next_u64() >> 11) * (1.0 / 9007199254740992.0)
```

The dataset is two blobs — class 0 centred at $(-2,-2)$, class 1 at $(+2,+2)$ — generated with
**arithmetic only** (no `sin`/`log`/`sqrt`), for the same reason: transcendental functions can
round differently across languages. The ideal separating line is $x_0 + x_1 = 0$.

### The unit and the learning rule

The model is three doubles. The forward pass is the pre-activation and the step; the learning
rule is written straight out. In C:

```c
typedef struct { double w0, w1, b; } Perceptron;

static double preactivation(const Perceptron *p, double x0, double x1) {
    return p->w0 * x0 + p->w1 * x1 + p->b;      /* z = w0*x0 + w1*x1 + b */
}
static int predict(const Perceptron *p, double x0, double x1) {
    return preactivation(p, x0, x1) >= 0.0 ? 1 : 0;   /* step(z) */
}

/* One pass of Rosenblatt's rule over all n samples, in fixed order. */
static void train_epoch(Perceptron *p, double lr,
                        double X[N_SAMPLES][N_FEATURES], int y[N_SAMPLES], int n) {
    for (int i = 0; i < n; i++) {
        int yhat = predict(p, X[i][0], X[i][1]);
        double err = (double)(y[i] - yhat);     /* -1, 0, or +1 */
        p->w0 += lr * err * X[i][0];
        p->w1 += lr * err * X[i][1];
        p->b  += lr * err;
    }
}
```

And the same thing in Python, line for line, so the two read side by side:

```python
def train_epoch(p, lr, X, y, n):
    """One pass of Rosenblatt's rule over all samples, fixed order (mirrors C)."""
    for i in range(n):
        x0, x1 = float(X[i, 0]), float(X[i, 1])
        yhat = p.predict(x0, x1)
        err = float(int(y[i]) - yhat)            # -1, 0, or +1
        p.w0 += lr * err * x0
        p.w1 += lr * err * x1
        p.b += lr * err
```

Train on the blobs with learning rate $\eta = 0.1$ for 20 epochs and the perceptron reaches
**100% accuracy** — the boundary settles into the separating line the theorem promised exists. A
small agreement test compiles the C, runs the Python, and asserts all eight tracked numbers
(the final $w_0, w_1, b$, and accuracy for both experiments) match to $10^{-9}$. That is what
makes "C and Python agree" a checkable fact rather than a hope.

## The wall: XOR

XOR — "exclusive or" — outputs 1 when its two inputs *differ* and 0 when they agree. Its truth
table is the simplest thing a single perceptron cannot do:

| $x_0$ | $x_1$ | XOR | needs the unit to… |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---|
| 0 | 0 | 0 | stay silent |
| 0 | 1 | 1 | fire |
| 1 | 0 | 1 | fire |
| 1 | 1 | 0 | stay silent |

The two "fire" points sit on one diagonal; the two "silent" points on the other. No straight line
can put both fire-points on one side and both silent-points on the other. Switch the widget above
to **XOR** and you can watch the perceptron confirm this the hard way — accuracy oscillates and
never reaches 1.

### A one-line proof of impossibility

We do not have to take the picture's word for it. Suppose some perceptron $(w_0, w_1, b)$ *did*
compute XOR. Then it must satisfy all four rows at once:

- $(0,0) \mapsto 0$: $\;b < 0.$
- $(1,0) \mapsto 1$: $\;w_0 + b \ge 0.$
- $(0,1) \mapsto 1$: $\;w_1 + b \ge 0.$
- $(1,1) \mapsto 0$: $\;w_0 + w_1 + b < 0.$

Add the two middle inequalities: $w_0 + w_1 + 2b \ge 0$, hence $w_0 + w_1 + b \ge -b$. But the
first line says $b < 0$, so $-b > 0$, which forces $w_0 + w_1 + b > 0$. That flatly contradicts
the fourth line, $w_0 + w_1 + b < 0$. No such perceptron exists. The failure is not about
training or luck — it is a theorem.

### The escape, foreshadowed

Notice that XOR *is* expressible with McCulloch–Pitts units — just not with **one**. XOR equals
"($x_0$ OR $x_1$) AND NOT ($x_0$ AND $x_1$)", a little circuit of the gates we built earlier.
Two layers of perceptrons can do it; a single layer cannot. The obstacle was never expressiveness
— it was **learning**. Rosenblatt's rule trains one layer. Nobody yet knew how to train the
*hidden* layer of a stack. That missing algorithm is the chain rule, and finding it is the whole
of the next chapter.

## The aftermath: the first winter

In 1969, Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert published *Perceptrons*, a rigorous mathematical
analysis of exactly what single-layer perceptrons can and cannot represent — with the XOR/parity
limitation at its centre. The book is often credited (fairly or not) with cooling enthusiasm and
funding for neural-network research through the 1970s. The connectionist program went quiet for
the better part of two decades.

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1958 | Rosenblatt's perceptron and the Mark I machine; the press hype |
| 1969 | Minsky & Papert, *Perceptrons* — the single-layer limit, proved |
| 1970s | Funding and interest recede — the connectionist "winter" |
| 1986 | Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams popularise **backpropagation** |

The thaw came in 1986, when Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams showed how to train the stacked
weights of a multilayer network by **back-propagating errors** — writing the chain rule out
across layers so that a hidden layer could learn too. XOR fell immediately. The line that could
not bend learned to bend.
