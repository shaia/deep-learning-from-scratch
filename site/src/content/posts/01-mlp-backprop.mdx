## The wall, and the way through

Module 00 ended at a theorem, not a bug. A single perceptron draws exactly one straight line, and
XOR — the tiny four-point problem where the two "fire" corners sit on one diagonal and the two
"silent" corners on the other — cannot be split by any line at all. We proved it in four
inequalities. No amount of training or luck escapes a theorem.

But we also noticed the escape hatch. XOR *is* expressible with the McCulloch–Pitts units we
already have — just not with **one**. It equals "($x_0$ OR $x_1$) AND NOT ($x_0$ AND $x_1$)": a
little circuit two layers deep. The obstacle was never expressiveness. It was **learning**.
Rosenblatt's rule trains one layer against a target. Nobody knew how to train the *hidden* layer
of a stack, because a hidden unit has no target of its own — no label says what it "should" have
output.

This chapter is the algorithm that supplies one. It has a name — **backpropagation** — and it is,
underneath, nothing but the chain rule from calculus, applied with bookkeeping. It is the single
most important algorithm in this entire book: every network that follows, up to the largest
language models, is trained by exactly this idea.

### 1986: learning the hidden layer

The multilayer network and even the rough idea of propagating errors backward had floated around
for years (Werbos had written it down in 1974). What changed in 1986 was clarity and momentum:
David Rumelhart, Geoffrey Hinton, and Ronald Williams published *Learning Representations by
Back-propagating Errors*, a short paper that stated the method cleanly, showed it *worked* on
problems like XOR, and — crucially — framed the hidden units as learning useful **internal
representations**. The connectionist winter that Minsky and Papert's *Perceptrons* had helped
bring on thawed almost overnight.

| Year | Milestone | What it added |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| 1969 | Minsky & Papert, *Perceptrons* | a proof of the single-layer wall (XOR) |
| 1974 | Werbos | the chain-rule idea, buried in a thesis |
| 1986 | Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams | backprop, stated clearly — and *it learns* |
| next | Module 02 | the tricks that make it train *fast* |

## The intuition: hidden units are learned features

Here is the whole idea in one sentence: **if one line is not enough, learn several, and then draw
a line in the space of their answers.**

Give the network a **hidden layer** of neurons. Each hidden unit is just a Module-00 neuron — a
weighted sum through a sigmoid — so each one carves the input plane with its own soft line. For
XOR, two hidden units are enough: one can learn "am I in the OR region?", the other "am I in the
AND region?". In the new 2D space whose coordinates are *those two answers*, the four XOR points
are no longer diagonal — they are linearly separable, and a single output neuron finishes the job.

The hidden layer, in other words, **learns a new set of features** — a coordinate system in which
the problem becomes easy. Nobody hand-designs those features; the network discovers them, because
backprop tells each hidden weight how it should change to reduce the final error. That is the
sentence Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams put at the centre of their paper, and it is why "deep
learning" is really *representation* learning.

## The math (all of it)

One hidden layer of $h$ sigmoid units. For an input $x \in \mathbb{R}^2$, with $\sigma(z) = 1/(1+e^{-z})$:

$$z_1 = W_1 x + b_1, \qquad a_1 = \sigma(z_1) \quad (\text{the } h \text{ hidden activations})$$
$$z_2 = W_2 a_1 + b_2, \qquad \hat{y} = a_2 = \sigma(z_2) \quad (\text{the output})$$

We measure error with **binary cross-entropy**, the natural loss for a probability output,
averaged over the $m$ training points:

$$L = -\frac{1}{m}\sum \Big[\, y \log a_2 + (1-y)\log(1-a_2) \,\Big].$$

### Backprop is the chain rule, output-first

To do gradient descent we need $\partial L / \partial \theta$ for every weight and bias $\theta$.
We get them by walking backward from the loss. The one fact worth committing to memory: for
cross-entropy through a sigmoid, the output error collapses to a plain difference. Write
$\delta_2 = \partial L / \partial z_2$; then

$$\delta_2 = a_2 - y.$$

The messy $\log$ and the sigmoid derivative cancel perfectly — a small miracle that is the reason
this pairing is standard. From $\delta_2$, every gradient is a local product. Using
$\sigma'(z) = a(1-a)$ for the hidden layer:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_2} = \delta_2\, a_1^\top, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial b_2} = \delta_2,$$
$$\delta_1 = \big(W_2^\top \delta_2\big) \odot a_1(1 - a_1), \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial W_1} = \delta_1\, x^\top, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial b_1} = \delta_1.$$

Read it as a flow: the error $\delta_2$ at the output is **pushed back** through $W_2$ to become
an error $\delta_1$ at the hidden layer (the $W_2^\top \delta_2$ term), then squeezed through the
hidden sigmoid's slope (the $a_1(1-a_1)$ term). Each layer hands the one before it a share of the
blame. Then we descend: $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\, \partial L / \partial \theta$.

That is the entire algorithm. The rest of this page is that written out in code — and then checked.

## The code: from scratch, and bit-for-bit

The project's contract (pedagogy principle #3) holds here too: the **C and Python implementations
must produce identical numbers** on the same seed. For XOR we write both with explicit loops in the
same order, initialize the weights from the same 64-bit LCG, and they agree to a relative
tolerance of $10^{-9}$. Here is the heart of it — the backward pass — side by side.

```python
# python/mlp.py — accumulate the gradient of the mean loss over all samples
def backward(self, X, y):
    n = len(y)
    dW1 = np.zeros_like(self.W1); db1 = np.zeros_like(self.b1)
    dW2 = np.zeros_like(self.W2); db2 = np.zeros_like(self.b2)
    for i in range(n):
        x0, x1, yi = float(X[i][0]), float(X[i][1]), float(y[i])
        a1, a2 = self.forward(x0, x1)
        dz2 = a2 - yi                          # BCE through sigmoid
        for j in range(self.n_hidden): dW2[j] += dz2 * a1[j]
        db2[0] += dz2
        for j in range(self.n_hidden):
            da1 = dz2 * float(self.W2[j])
            dz1 = da1 * a1[j] * (1.0 - a1[j])  # sigmoid'
            dW1[j, 0] += dz1 * x0; dW1[j, 1] += dz1 * x1
            db1[j] += dz1
    dW1 /= n; db1 /= n; dW2 /= n; db2 /= n
    return dW1, db1, dW2, db2
```

```c
/* c/mlp.c — the same chain rule, same order, so the two agree bit-for-bit */
double dz2 = a2 - yi;
for (int j = 0; j < N_HIDDEN; j++)
    dW2[j] += dz2 * a1[j];                         /* dL/dW2_j = dz2 * a1_j */
db2 += dz2;
for (int j = 0; j < N_HIDDEN; j++) {
    double da1 = dz2 * m->W2[j];                   /* push back through W2 */
    double dz1 = da1 * a1[j] * (1.0 - a1[j]);      /* through sigmoid'     */
    dW1[j][0] += dz1 * x0; dW1[j][1] += dz1 * x1;
    db1[j] += dz1;
}
```

### The gradient check: how we *know* it is right

Hand-derived gradients are exactly where bugs hide — a dropped $\sigma'$ factor, a wrong sign, a
transposed index. From this module on, every backward pass earns a **finite-difference gradient
check**. Nudge one weight by $\pm h$, watch the loss, and estimate the slope numerically:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta} \approx \frac{L(\theta + h) - L(\theta - h)}{2h}.$$

If the analytic gradient matches this numerical one to a relative error near $10^{-7}$, the
derivation is correct — no reasoning required, just arithmetic. Our agreement test runs it on all
four parameter tensors, and it passes ($\text{rel\_error} \in [4\times 10^{-10},\, 10^{-7}]$).
The notebook goes one further and checks the same gradients against **PyTorch's** autograd, which
agrees to $\sim 10^{-15}$ — machine precision. The hand-written chain rule and the industrial
autodiff engine compute the same thing, because there is only one right answer.

## Toy to real: 97% on MNIST

XOR proves the mechanism; MNIST proves it *scales*. The same network — now `784 → 128 → 10` with a
**softmax** output over the ten digits — trains on 60,000 handwritten images with plain
mini-batch SGD and **no tricks** (initialization, momentum, and regularization are Module 02). It
crosses 95% within two epochs and settles near **97.7%**. The softmax's cross-entropy gradient has
the *same* clean form we derived for XOR, $\delta_2 = a_2 - y$ (now a vector minus a one-hot), so
the backward pass is the batched twin of the code above. The
[notebook](https://github.com/shaia/deep-learning-from-scratch/blob/main/topics/01-mlp-backprop/notebook.ipynb)
trains it end to end and cross-checks the result against TensorFlow and Keras.
