## The wall, and the way through

Module 00 ended at a theorem, not a bug. A single perceptron draws exactly one straight line, and
XOR — the tiny four-point problem where the two "fire" corners sit on one diagonal and the two
"silent" corners on the other — cannot be split by any line at all. We proved it in four
inequalities. No amount of training or luck escapes a theorem.

But we also noticed the escape hatch. XOR *is* expressible with the McCulloch–Pitts units we
already have — just not with **one**. It equals "($x_0$ OR $x_1$) AND NOT ($x_0$ AND $x_1$)": a
little circuit two layers deep. The obstacle was never expressiveness. It was **learning**.
Rosenblatt's rule trains one layer against a target. Nobody knew how to train the *hidden* layer
of a stack, because a hidden unit has no target of its own — no label says what it "should" have
output.

This chapter is the algorithm that supplies one. It has a name — **backpropagation** — and it is,
underneath, nothing but the chain rule from calculus, applied with bookkeeping. But to make the
chain rule applicable at all, three other ideas have to arrive with it: a *smooth* neuron in
place of Module 00's all-or-none step, a single number — a **loss** — that measures how wrong
the whole network is, and **gradient descent**, the discipline of nudging every weight downhill
on that number. Together they form the framework that every remaining chapter of this book lives
inside. Every network that follows, up to the largest language models, is trained by exactly the
machinery we are about to build by hand.

### An algorithm discovered four times

The strange thing about backpropagation is not that it was discovered — it is how many times it
had to be discovered before anyone listened. The chain rule itself is three centuries old. The
efficient trick at backprop's core — sweep *backward* through a composition, reusing shared
subexpressions, so that all the derivatives together cost about as much as one forward pass —
was published in 1970 by Seppo Linnainmaa, a Finnish master's student writing about the
accumulation of rounding errors. His "reverse mode of automatic differentiation" is exactly
backprop's engine, but it said nothing about neurons, and nobody training networks read it.

In 1974 Paul Werbos put the pieces together explicitly. His Harvard PhD thesis, *Beyond
Regression*, proposed training multilayer perceptrons by propagating derivatives backward — the
whole idea, worked out, a decade early. It landed in silence. This was the depth of the
connectionist winter; Minsky and Papert's *Perceptrons* had convinced the field that the whole
direction was a dead end, and Werbos later recalled being unable to persuade anyone — including
Minsky himself — that the two-layer objection had an answer. The result stayed buried in a
thesis for ten years. In the mid-1980s the idea surfaced twice more, independently: David Parker
in an MIT technical report (1985), and Yann LeCun — whom we will meet again in Module 03 — in a
French conference paper the same year.

What changed in 1986 was not the mathematics but the delivery. David Rumelhart, Geoffrey Hinton,
and Ronald Williams — working within the *Parallel Distributed Processing* group, whose
two-volume book that year re-founded connectionism — published a short paper in *Nature*
titled *Learning Representations by Back-propagating Errors*. It stated the method cleanly,
and, crucially, it *demonstrated* it. One demonstration became famous. They trained a network on
two family trees — one English, one Italian, identical in structure — feeding it triples like
"Colin's father is…" and asking it to fill in the person. Then they looked inside. Individual
hidden units, which no one had programmed, had learned to encode *nationality*, *generation*,
and *branch of the family* — precisely the features a human analyst would invent to solve the
task. That is what the title meant: the hidden layer does not just compute, it learns
**internal representations**. The framing stuck, the winter thawed, and "learning
representations" became — and remains — the deepest one-line summary of what this field does.

| Year | Milestone | What it added |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| 1969 | Minsky & Papert, *Perceptrons* | a proof of the single-layer wall (XOR) |
| 1970 | Linnainmaa | reverse-mode differentiation — the engine, minus the neurons |
| 1974 | Werbos, *Beyond Regression* | backprop for networks, buried in a thesis |
| 1985 | Parker; LeCun | two independent rediscoveries |
| 1986 | Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams | stated cleanly, shown to work, framed as *representation learning* |

## The intuition: hidden units are learned features

Here is the whole idea in one sentence: **if one line is not enough, learn several, and then draw
a line in the space of their answers.**

Give the network a **hidden layer** of neurons. Each hidden unit is just a Module-00 neuron — a
weighted sum through a squashing function — so each one carves the input plane with its own soft
line. For XOR, two hidden units are enough: one can learn "am I in the OR region?", the other "am
I in the AND region?". Now hand the *output* neuron not the raw inputs but those two answers. In
the new 2D space whose coordinates are the hidden units' outputs, the four XOR points are no
longer diagonal — they are linearly separable, and a single output neuron finishes the job.

The hidden layer, in other words, **learns a new set of features** — a coordinate system in which
the problem becomes easy. Nobody hand-designs those features; the network discovers them, exactly
as the family-tree network discovered nationality and generation, because backprop tells each
hidden weight how it should change to reduce the final error. That is why "deep learning" is
really *representation* learning. Later in this chapter we will watch it happen live — the
widget below replots every data point in the hidden layer's learned coordinates, and you can see
the four XOR corners physically untangle. But first we owe ourselves the machinery: what,
precisely, tells a hidden weight which way to move?

## The new rules of the game

Rosenblatt's rule needed no calculus. It looked at one unit's error — $-1$, $0$, or $+1$ — and
shoved the weights accordingly. That works precisely because the unit being corrected has a
target to be wrong about. A hidden unit has none, so the mistake-driven style dies at the first
hidden layer. The 1986 solution replaces it with a scheme that needs no per-unit targets at all,
built from three commitments.

### Why the step function had to go

The plan is to ask, for every weight in the network, "if I nudged this weight a hair, would the
final error go up or down?" — and that question is a *derivative*. Module 00's step function is
the enemy of that question. Its graph is two flat shelves: the derivative is $0$ everywhere (and
undefined at the jump). Nudge a weight a hair and the unit's output changes *not at all* — until
suddenly it snaps. Flat shelves mean zero signal: no blame can flow backward through a step.

So we swap the step for its smooth cousin, the **sigmoid**:

$$
\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}.
$$

It has the same character — near $0$ for very negative $z$, near $1$ for very positive $z$, its
decisive swing around the threshold — but every point of it has a usable slope. And it buys a
bonus: since the output now lives *between* $0$ and $1$, we can read it as a **probability**.
Module 00's unit shouted "fire" or "silent"; this one murmurs "$0.93$ sure". Keep that
interpretation in hand — it is about to choose our loss function for us.

### One number to make small

The second commitment: measure the whole network's wrongness with a single scalar, the **loss**
$L$, averaged over training examples. Imagine $L$ as a landscape over the space of all weights —
every possible setting of every $W$ and $b$ is a point, and the altitude there is how badly the
network does. Learning is descending this landscape.

Calculus hands us the compass. The **gradient** $\partial L / \partial \theta$, taken for every
parameter $\theta$, points in the direction of steepest *ascent* — so we step against it:

$$
\theta \;\leftarrow\; \theta - \eta\, \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta},
$$

with the **learning rate** $\eta$ setting the stride. That is **gradient descent**. Notice what
changed philosophically since Module 00: the perceptron rule corrected one unit's mistake on one
point; gradient descent optimizes a *global* objective, and every weight — hidden or not — gets
its marching orders from the same scalar. A hidden unit needs no target of its own anymore. It
only needs its share of $\partial L$, and computing those shares efficiently is the whole of
backpropagation. This loss-landscape-plus-gradient framework, not any particular architecture, is
the constant of this book: every chapter from here to the Transformer changes the *shape* of the
function being descended, never the descent.

### Why cross-entropy, and not squared error

Which loss? The obvious first guess is squared error, $L = \tfrac12(a_2 - y)^2$ — it worked for
centuries of curve fitting. But watch what it does through a sigmoid output. By the chain rule
(we will earn this formula properly in a moment):

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_2} = (a_2 - y)\,\sigma'(z_2).
$$

The factor $\sigma'(z_2)$ is the sigmoid's slope, and the sigmoid is *flat* at both ends. Suppose
the network is confidently wrong: $y = 1$ but $a_2 \approx 0$. Then $z_2$ sits far out on the
flat left shelf, $\sigma'(z_2) \approx 0$, and the gradient — error times slope — is almost
nothing. Squared error learns *slowest* exactly when it is *most* wrong. That is a design flaw,
not a nuisance.

The fix takes the probability reading of $a_2$ seriously. If the network outputs the probability
of class $1$, the natural objective is to make the *probability it assigns to the correct label*
as large as possible — equivalently, to minimize the negative log of it. Averaged over $m$
examples, that is **binary cross-entropy**:

$$
L = -\frac{1}{m}\sum \Big[\, y \log a_2 + (1-y)\log(1-a_2) \,\Big].
$$

(Read the bracket case by case: when $y=1$ it is $\log a_2$, when $y=0$ it is $\log(1-a_2)$ —
always the log-probability of what actually happened.) Cross-entropy's punishment grows without
bound as the model gets confidently wrong — and, as we are about to derive, its gradient
*cancels* the sigmoid's flat shelves exactly. The two were made for each other.

## The math (all of it)

Fix the architecture: one hidden layer of $h$ sigmoid units. For an input $x \in \mathbb{R}^2$:

$$
z_1 = W_1 x + b_1, \qquad a_1 = \sigma(z_1) \quad (\text{the } h \text{ hidden activations})
$$

$$
z_2 = W_2 a_1 + b_2, \qquad \hat{y} = a_2 = \sigma(z_2) \quad (\text{the output}),
$$

with binary cross-entropy as the loss. To run gradient descent we need
$\partial L / \partial \theta$ for all four parameter tensors $W_1, b_1, W_2, b_2$. We will build
the answer in four steps, deriving everything: the sigmoid's slope, the chain rule on a single
path, the output error, and finally the full network — at any depth.

### Step 1: the sigmoid's slope

Differentiate $\sigma(z) = (1 + e^{-z})^{-1}$ with the quotient rule:

$$
\sigma'(z) = \frac{e^{-z}}{(1+e^{-z})^2}
= \underbrace{\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}}}_{\sigma(z)} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{e^{-z}}{1+e^{-z}}}_{1 - \sigma(z)}
= \sigma(z)\,\big(1 - \sigma(z)\big).
$$

Two things to notice. Practically, the derivative is computable from the activation alone — if
you kept $a = \sigma(z)$ from the forward pass, the slope is just $a(1-a)$, no re-exponentiation
needed; every implementation in this book exploits that. And structurally: $a(1-a)$ peaks at
$\tfrac14$ when $a = \tfrac12$ and decays to $0$ at both ends. The slope is *at most a quarter*.
File that fact away — it returns at the end of this chapter with consequences.

### Step 2: the chain rule, on one path

Strip the network to its skeleton: one input, one hidden unit, one output — a chain
$x \to z_1 \to a_1 \to z_2 \to a_2 \to L$, where $z_1 = w_1 x + b_1$ and $z_2 = w_2 a_1 + b_2$.
How does the loss respond to a wiggle in the *first* weight, $w_1$? The wiggle propagates:
nudging $w_1$ moves $z_1$, which moves $a_1$ through the sigmoid, which moves $z_2$ through
$w_2$, which moves $a_2$ through the other sigmoid, which moves $L$. The chain rule says the
total sensitivity is the product of the local ones:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1}
= \frac{\partial L}{\partial a_2}
\cdot \underbrace{\sigma'(z_2)}_{a_2 \to z_2}
\cdot \underbrace{w_2}_{z_2 \to a_1}
\cdot \underbrace{\sigma'(z_1)}_{a_1 \to z_1}
\cdot \underbrace{x}_{z_1 \to w_1}.
$$

Every factor is *local* — each arrow of the chain contributes its own slope, and nothing else.
That locality is the entire secret. In a real network many weights share the front of this
product (everything from $L$ down to their own layer), so we compute it once, from the output
backward, and hand it down. The bookkeeping gets a name: write

$$
\delta_\ell \;=\; \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_\ell}
$$

for the loss's sensitivity to layer $\ell$'s pre-activation — the "**error at layer $\ell$**".
Backpropagation is nothing but computing $\delta$ at the output and passing it backward, one
local factor at a time.

### Step 3: the small miracle at the output

Start the backward walk at the loss. For one example, $L = -[\,y \log a_2 + (1-y)\log(1-a_2)\,]$,
so, differentiating each term,

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial a_2} = -\frac{y}{a_2} + \frac{1-y}{1-a_2}
= \frac{-y(1-a_2) + (1-y)\,a_2}{a_2(1-a_2)}
= \frac{a_2 - y}{a_2(1-a_2)}.
$$

An ugly fraction — until we take the next step of the chain, through the output sigmoid, whose
slope we derived in Step 1 to be exactly $a_2(1-a_2)$:

$$
\delta_2 = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_2}
= \frac{\partial L}{\partial a_2}\cdot\sigma'(z_2)
= \frac{a_2 - y}{a_2(1-a_2)} \cdot a_2(1-a_2)
= a_2 - y.
$$

The denominator and the sigmoid slope annihilate each other. What survives is the cleanest
possible error signal: *prediction minus target*. No flat shelves, no vanishing factor — a
confidently-wrong output ($y=1$, $a_2 \approx 0$) gets a gradient of nearly $-1$, full strength.
This is not luck; cross-entropy is precisely the loss whose steepness grows in step with the
sigmoid's flatness, so their product stays honest. The pairing is standard everywhere for exactly
this reason, and $\delta_2 = a_2 - y$ is the one line of this chapter most worth memorizing.

### Step 4: the full backward pass

Now the real network, where $W_1$ is $h \times 2$ and the hidden layer is a vector. Nothing new
happens — we apply the same local factors, just for all units at once. From $\delta_2$, the
output layer's own gradients are immediate, because $z_2 = W_2 a_1 + b_2$ is linear in its
parameters (the local slope of $z_2$ with respect to $W_2$ is just $a_1$, and with respect to
$b_2$ is $1$):

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_2} = \delta_2\, a_1^\top, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial b_2} = \delta_2.
$$

To keep walking backward we need the error at the hidden layer. Two local steps: from $z_2$ back
to $a_1$ (the local factor is the weight matrix — each hidden unit $j$ influenced $z_2$ in
proportion to $W_{2,j}$), then from $a_1$ back to $z_1$ (the local factor is the hidden sigmoid's
slope, elementwise):

$$
\delta_1 = \big(W_2^\top \delta_2\big) \odot a_1(1 - a_1),
$$

and then the hidden layer's parameter gradients mirror the output layer's:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_1} = \delta_1\, x^\top, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial b_1} = \delta_1.
$$

Read it as a flow: the error $\delta_2$ at the output is **pushed back** through $W_2$ to become
an error $\delta_1$ at the hidden layer, then squeezed through the hidden sigmoid's slope. Each
layer hands the one before it a share of the blame, weighted by how much it listened. Then every
parameter descends: $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\, \partial L / \partial \theta$.

### Backprop at any depth

The claim in this chapter's opening — that the largest language models train by *exactly this* —
deserves its equations. For a network of $L$ layers, with $a^{(0)} = x$ and
$z^{(\ell)} = W^{(\ell)} a^{(\ell-1)} + b^{(\ell)}$, the backward pass is a four-line recursion:

$$
\delta^{(L)} = a^{(L)} - y \qquad \text{(cross-entropy output, as in Step 3)}
$$

$$
\delta^{(\ell)} = \big(W^{(\ell+1)\top}\, \delta^{(\ell+1)}\big) \odot \sigma'\!\big(z^{(\ell)}\big)
\qquad \ell = L-1, \dots, 1
$$

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial W^{(\ell)}} = \delta^{(\ell)}\, a^{(\ell-1)\top}, \qquad
\frac{\partial L}{\partial b^{(\ell)}} = \delta^{(\ell)}.
$$

One forward sweep stores every activation; one backward sweep computes every gradient. The
backward sweep costs the same order of work as the forward one — that efficiency, Linnainmaa's
1970 observation, is what makes training million- and billion-parameter models thinkable at all.
Deeper chapters will change what a "layer" is — convolutions in Module 03, recurrence in
Module 06, attention in Module 13 — and every one of those layers simply supplies its own local
factors to this same recursion. Learn these four lines once and you have learned how every
network in this book trains.

> **Aside: how much can one hidden layer do?** In 1989 George Cybenko proved that a network with
> a *single* hidden layer of sigmoid units can approximate any continuous function on a bounded
> region to any desired accuracy, given enough hidden units — the **universal approximation
> theorem**. So why isn't this book one chapter long? Because the theorem promises *existence*,
> not *learnability*: it says a good network is out there, not that gradient descent will find
> it, and not that "enough units" is a number you can afford. The rest of deep learning — depth,
> architecture, initialization, optimization — is the study of making good networks *findable*.
> Existence was never the hard part.

## The geometry: what the hidden layer learns

Module 00 taught us to see one neuron geometrically: its weight vector is the normal of a line,
and its output says which side of the line you are on — softly, now that the step is a sigmoid.
A hidden layer of $h$ units is therefore $h$ *soft lines* laid across the input plane, and the
hidden activation vector $a_1$ is a point's **coordinates relative to those lines**: unit $j$'s
activation answers "how far past line $j$ is this point?", squashed to $(0,1)$. The map
$x \mapsto a_1$ is a learned change of coordinates, and the output unit is an ordinary
Module-00 perceptron living in that new space.

That is the picture to hold while playing with the widget below. The left panel is the familiar
input plane. The right panel is the *hidden space*: every data point replotted at the coordinates
the hidden layer assigns it, with the output unit's straight cut shown when it can be drawn
exactly. Press **Play** on two moons and watch both panels at once — as the input-space boundary
bends, the hidden-space points drift apart until a straight line separates what no straight line
could separate on the left. Then switch to **XOR**, set **hidden** to **2**, and press Play. The
four corners — provably inseparable where they started — physically untangle: the two hidden
units learn their two soft lines (one hunting the OR region, one the AND region, just as the
intuition section promised), and in the coordinates of *their answers*, the diagonal knot comes
undone. Finally drag **hidden** down to **1**: the hidden space collapses to a line, the network
loses its power to bend, and XOR becomes impossible again. That knob *is* Minsky and Papert's
theorem, and the escape from it, in one slider.

## The code: from scratch, and bit-for-bit

The project's contract (pedagogy principle #3) holds here too: the **C and Python implementations
must produce identical numbers** on the same seed. For XOR we write both with explicit loops in
the same order, initialize the weights from the same 64-bit LCG we built in Module 00, and they
agree to a relative tolerance of $10^{-9}$. The code walks the same order as the math: initialize,
forward, backward.

### Starting weights: why zero no longer works

Module 00's perceptron could start from $w = 0$ and learn fine. An MLP **cannot**, and the reason
is a lovely symmetry trap. Suppose every weight starts at zero (or at any shared constant). Then
all $h$ hidden units compute the same $z_1$, hence the same activation; the backward pass hands
them all the same $\delta_1$ component; so they all receive the *same update* and remain
identical copies of one another — forever. Gradient descent preserves the symmetry it starts
with. An $h$-unit hidden layer initialized to zeros is, and will always remain, a one-unit layer
in disguise, and XOR needs at least two genuinely different units. So we must **break the
symmetry** by starting random:

```c
/* Initialize weights by drawing from the RNG in a fixed order: W1 row-major,
 * then b1, then W2, then b2 -- the exact order the Python mirror uses. */
static void mlp_init(MLP *m, Rng *r, double scale) {
    for (int j = 0; j < N_HIDDEN; j++)
        for (int k = 0; k < N_IN; k++)
            m->W1[j][k] = scale * rng_signed(r);
    for (int j = 0; j < N_HIDDEN; j++)
        m->b1[j] = scale * rng_signed(r);
    for (int j = 0; j < N_HIDDEN; j++)
        m->W2[j] = scale * rng_signed(r);
    m->b2 = scale * rng_signed(r);
}
```

Each weight is a uniform draw in $[-\text{scale}, \text{scale}]$, and the Python mirror consumes
the identical RNG stream in the identical order, so both languages start from the same network.
How *large* the random values should be is a surprisingly deep question — too big saturates the
sigmoids, too small starves them — but it is Module 02's question; here a scale of $1.0$ serves.

### Forward: run the network

The forward pass is the two layer equations, written as loops. In C:

```c
/* Forward pass: input -> hidden (sigmoid) -> output (sigmoid). Fills a1[] with
 * the hidden activations and returns a2 = yhat. */
static double forward(const MLP *m, double x0, double x1, double a1[N_HIDDEN]) {
    for (int j = 0; j < N_HIDDEN; j++) {
        double z1 = m->b1[j] + m->W1[j][0] * x0 + m->W1[j][1] * x1;
        a1[j] = sigmoid(z1);                 /* a1_j = sigmoid(z1_j) */
    }
    double z2 = m->b2;
    for (int j = 0; j < N_HIDDEN; j++)
        z2 += m->W2[j] * a1[j];              /* z2 = W2 . a1 + b2 */
    return sigmoid(z2);                      /* yhat = sigmoid(z2) */
}
```

Note that it *returns the hidden activations*, not just the answer — the backward pass needs
$a_1$ both for $\partial L/\partial W_2$ and for the slope factor $a_1(1-a_1)$, so the forward
pass saves its work. (Every framework you will ever use does the same; the saved activations are
why training takes more memory than inference.) The Python mirror reads line for line the same.

### Backward: the chain rule, verbatim

Here is the heart of the module — the backward pass, side by side. Compare each line to Step 4's
equations; the correspondence is one-to-one.

```python
# python/mlp.py — accumulate the gradient of the mean loss over all samples
def backward(self, X, y):
    n = len(y)
    dW1 = np.zeros_like(self.W1); db1 = np.zeros_like(self.b1)
    dW2 = np.zeros_like(self.W2); db2 = np.zeros_like(self.b2)
    for i in range(n):
        x0, x1, yi = float(X[i][0]), float(X[i][1]), float(y[i])
        a1, a2 = self.forward(x0, x1)
        dz2 = a2 - yi                          # step 3: delta2 = a2 - y
        for j in range(self.n_hidden): dW2[j] += dz2 * a1[j]
        db2[0] += dz2
        for j in range(self.n_hidden):
            da1 = dz2 * float(self.W2[j])      # push back through W2
            dz1 = da1 * a1[j] * (1.0 - a1[j])  # through sigmoid' = a(1-a)
            dW1[j, 0] += dz1 * x0; dW1[j, 1] += dz1 * x1
            db1[j] += dz1
    dW1 /= n; db1 /= n; dW2 /= n; db2 /= n
    return dW1, db1, dW2, db2
```

```c
/* c/mlp.c — the same chain rule, same order, so the two agree bit-for-bit */
double dz2 = a2 - yi;
for (int j = 0; j < N_HIDDEN; j++)
    dW2[j] += dz2 * a1[j];                         /* dL/dW2_j = dz2 * a1_j */
db2 += dz2;
for (int j = 0; j < N_HIDDEN; j++) {
    double da1 = dz2 * m->W2[j];                   /* push back through W2 */
    double dz1 = da1 * a1[j] * (1.0 - a1[j]);      /* through sigmoid'     */
    dW1[j][0] += dz1 * x0; dW1[j][1] += dz1 * x1;
    db1[j] += dz1;
}
```

### From loops to matrices

The loops are the honest teaching form, but they hide a fact the math already told us: each of
those inner loops *is* a matrix operation. `python/mlp.py` also carries a vectorized twin,
`MLPVec`, that processes a whole **batch** of examples at once — inputs stacked as rows of a
matrix $X$ — by turning every loop into a matrix product:

```python
def backward(self, X, y):
    n = X.shape[0]
    P = self.forward(X)
    Y = np.zeros_like(P)
    Y[np.arange(n), y] = 1.0
    dZ2 = (P - Y) / n                      # delta2, for all n samples at once
    dW2 = self.A1.T @ dZ2                  # delta2 a1^T, summed over the batch
    db2 = dZ2.sum(axis=0)
    dA1 = dZ2 @ self.W2.T                  # push back through W2
    dZ1 = dA1 * self.A1 * (1.0 - self.A1)  # through sigmoid'
    dW1 = X.T @ dZ1
    db1 = dZ1.sum(axis=0)
    return dW1, db1, dW2, db2
```

Same five equations, no loops in sight: `@` sums over what the loops iterated. This matters for
two reasons. Pedagogically, it proves the matrix equations of Step 4 *are* the loop code — if you
ever lose your footing in a framework's tensor expressions, expand one back into its loops.
Practically, matrix multiplication is what CPUs and GPUs are built to do fast, so the batched
form is how every serious system computes — and it is what we will need to face 60,000 images in
a moment.

## The gradient check: how we *know* it is right

Hand-derived gradients are exactly where bugs hide — a dropped $\sigma'$ factor, a wrong sign, a
transposed index — and a subtly wrong gradient still *sort of* learns, which makes it the worst
kind of bug: invisible. So from this module on, every backward pass in this book earns a
**finite-difference gradient check**. The idea is the definition of the derivative, used as a
witness. Nudge one parameter by $\pm h$, run the *forward* pass twice, and estimate the slope
numerically:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta} \approx \frac{L(\theta + h) - L(\theta - h)}{2h}.
$$

The estimate needs no calculus, no chain rule — only the ability to compute the loss. If the
analytic gradient from our backward pass matches it to a relative error near $10^{-7}$ (the
noise floor of the estimate itself in double precision), the derivation is correct: no reasoning
required, just arithmetic. Our agreement test runs the check on all four parameter tensors:

```
dW1  rel_error = 1.03e-07  ok
db1  rel_error = 2.95e-09  ok
dW2  rel_error = 5.62e-10  ok
db2  rel_error = 4.23e-10  ok
```

The notebook goes one further and loads our exact weights into **PyTorch**, whose autograd
engine re-derives the gradients by its own means: agreement to $\sim 10^{-15}$ — machine
precision. The hand-written chain rule and the industrial autodiff engine compute the same thing,
because there is only one right answer.

## Toy to real: 97% on MNIST

XOR proves the mechanism on four points. The book's rule (pedagogy principle #4) is that no
mechanism gets to stop there: it must face a real dataset, where success is a measurement rather
than a demonstration. Ours is **MNIST**.

### Meet the dataset

MNIST is 70,000 handwritten digits — 60,000 for training, 10,000 held out for testing — each a
$28 \times 28$ grayscale image, which we flatten to a vector of $784$ pixel intensities. It was
assembled in the 1990s by Yann LeCun, Corinna Cortes, and Christopher Burges from two batches of
U.S. digit forms collected by NIST — one written by Census Bureau employees, one by high-school
students — remixed so the training and test sets share a distribution ("Modified NIST"). This
was no toy to its makers: LeCun's group was building the networks that would read real ZIP
codes on envelopes and real amounts on checks, and MNIST became *the* benchmark of 1990s
neural network research — it has been called the drosophila of machine learning, small enough
to run in minutes, real enough that results on it mean something. It will be our companion for
several chapters, so the numbers we set here matter — they are the baseline every later
architecture must beat.

### From one probability to ten: softmax

XOR needed one probability; MNIST needs a verdict across **ten classes**. The output layer grows
to ten units, and we need their ten pre-activations turned into ten probabilities that sum to
one. The standard tool is **softmax** — exponentiate each score to make it positive, then
normalize:

$$
p_k = \frac{e^{z_k}}{\sum_{j} e^{z_j}}, \qquad k = 0, \dots, 9.
$$

The loss is cross-entropy again, now in its multi-class form: minimize the negative log of the
probability given to the correct class, $L = -\log p_y$. And here the chapter's central
cancellation performs an encore. Since $\log p_y = z_y - \log \sum_j e^{z_j}$, differentiating
with respect to score $z_k$ gives $\partial \log p_y / \partial z_k = y_k - p_k$, where $y$ is
the one-hot vector for the true class ($y_k = 1$ if $k$ is the right digit, else $0$). So

$$
\delta_2 = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_2} = p - y
$$

— *prediction minus target*, again. The messy exponentials and the log flatten each other, just
as sigmoid and BCE did in Step 3, and for the same underlying reason. Every line of our backward
pass survives the move to ten classes untouched; only the output width changed.

### Mini-batches, and the run

One last idea and we can train. On XOR we descended the gradient of the loss over all four
points — *full-batch* descent. On 60,000 images, computing the exact gradient for every single
step is wasteful: an estimate from a small random sample of examples points in nearly the same
direction and costs a thousandth as much. So each epoch we shuffle the training set, split it
into **mini-batches** of 64, and take one gradient step per batch — 938 slightly-noisy steps per
epoch instead of one exact one. This is **stochastic gradient descent** (SGD), the workhorse of
everything to come, and the batched matrix code above is exactly what makes it cheap.

The network is the same shape as XOR's, scaled: $784 \to 128 \to 10$, sigmoid hidden layer,
softmax output, initialized with a modest $1/\sqrt{\text{fan-in}}$ scaling to keep the sigmoids
responsive (Module 02 will justify that choice). No momentum, no regularization, no schedule —
plain SGD, because this chapter's claim is that the *mechanism alone* suffices. Here is the run:

Look closely and the mistakes split in two. Some digits are simply terrible handwriting — a human
would hesitate too, and the residual disagreement between careful humans on MNIST is around
0.2%. But others look easy, and those failures share a cause we built in on purpose: we fed the
network a *flat vector* of 784 numbers, throwing away the fact that the image was
two-dimensional. Our MLP does not know that pixel 29 sits directly below pixel 1; a stroke
shifted two pixels left is, to it, an entirely different input. For calibration: a straight-line
model — softmax regression, no hidden layer at all — manages only about 88–92% on MNIST, so the
hidden layer's learned features bought us a five-fold cut in errors. Closing most of the
remaining gap requires teaching the network about *space itself* — that is convolution, and it is
Module 03's story.

## What 1986 didn't solve

Module 00 ended with a winter; honesty requires admitting this chapter does not end with an
endless summer. Backpropagation removed the *in-principle* barrier — any shape of network,
trainable — and yet by the mid-1990s neural networks had slipped out of fashion again, losing
benchmark after benchmark to newer methods. If backprop works, why did the field stall a second
time? Because in practice, four problems were waiting, and we have already met the seeds of each.

The worst hides in Step 1's innocent fact that $\sigma' \le \tfrac14$. Look again at the
general recursion: each layer's $\delta$ is the next layer's $\delta$ *multiplied by* a weight
term and by $\sigma'(z)$. Stack many layers and the error signal is multiplied by a
quarter-or-less at every hop — by layer ten, the front of the network receives an exponentially
faint whisper of the blame. This is the **vanishing gradient problem** (named and analyzed by
Sepp Hochreiter in 1991), and it meant that "deep" networks — the whole promise of the idea —
mostly refused to train. Saturation makes it worse: a unit pushed onto its flat shelf has
$\sigma' \approx 0$ and goes silent for good. Second, our results were quietly fragile: a
hand-tuned learning rate, a hand-picked initialization scale — nudge either and XOR fails to
converge; nobody yet knew principled choices. Third, gradient descent on a non-convex landscape
carries no promise: Module 00's convergence *theorem* is gone, replaced by "follow the slope and
hope", and the era's theorists feared the hills were full of traps. And fourth, the compute and
data that would eventually dissolve most of these worries — GPUs, web-scale datasets — were
decades away. Kernel machines, with convex losses and real guarantees, deservedly won the 1990s.

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1986 | Backprop popularized — any architecture trainable *in principle* |
| 1989 | Universal approximation proved; LeCun's group starts reading real ZIP codes |
| 1991 | Hochreiter names the vanishing gradient — depth doesn't train |
| mid-1990s | SVMs and friends win the benchmarks; the second neural lull |
| 2006–2012 | Better init, better activations, GPUs, and data end the lull (Modules 02–04) |

Every one of those four problems has since been solved or tamed, and the solutions are precisely
the next chapters: initialization and activations that keep gradients alive, optimizers that tune
their own step sizes, regularization that manages the train–test gap we just witnessed
(Module 02); architectures that respect the structure of images (Module 03); and, much later,
skip connections that let gradients bypass the multiplication chain entirely (Module 05). The
machine works. Making it work *fast, deep, and reliably* is the story from here.
